SCENARIO 1
The Contract Dispute
Scenario
Facts

A supply agreement contains three clauses:

Clause 1: "If payment is received by the 5th of the month, delivery will occur by the 10th."

Clause 2: "Delivery will occur by the 10th if and only if the goods have cleared customs."

Clause 3: "If delivery does not occur by the 10th, the buyer may claim a penalty."

Payment was received on the 3rd. Delivery did not occur by the 10th. The supplier argues no penalty is owed because the goods did not clear customs.

Questions
T2 Using Clause 1 alone, what was the supplier obligated to do given that payment was received on the 3rd?
Clause 1: p → q (payment by 5th → delivery by 10th)
Payment on 3rd: p = T
By Modus Ponens: q = T — delivery by 10th was obligated

Clause 1 created an obligation to deliver by the 10th once payment was received by the 5th. The supplier was obligated to deliver.

Modus Ponens · conditional obligation
T2 Clause 2 uses "if and only if." What does this add that a simple conditional would not? Does the customs failure affect the Clause 1 obligation?
Clause 2: D ↔ C (delivery by 10th iff customs cleared)
Clause 1: P → D (payment by 5th → delivery by 10th)

What the biconditional adds: Clause 2 makes customs clearance both necessary and sufficient for delivery. A simple conditional (C → D) would only say customs clearance is sufficient — delivery could occur some other way. The biconditional closes that: no delivery without customs, no customs without delivery. They are logically equivalent.

Does customs failure affect the Clause 1 obligation? This is the crux. Clause 1 creates an obligation (P → D). Clause 2 creates a factual condition (D ↔ C). If customs clearance is genuinely impossible, then D cannot occur — but the question is whether the supplier undertook to ensure customs clearance as part of the Clause 1 obligation, or whether customs is a force majeure condition outside the contract. Logic alone cannot resolve this — it requires contractual interpretation of whether the obligation is absolute or conditional on customs.

Biconditional vs. conditional · necessary and sufficient conditions · the limits of logic in contractual interpretation
T3 The supplier argues: "Delivery didn't happen, but that's because customs didn't clear — so no penalty." Reconstruct this as a formal argument, identify its logical form, and assess whether it is valid. What is the buyer's strongest logical counter?
Supplier's argument:
P1: D ↔ C (Clause 2 — delivery iff customs cleared)
P2: ¬C (customs not cleared)
P3: P → D (Clause 1 — payment triggers delivery obligation)
P4: P (payment made)
Supplier claims: ¬penalty despite ¬D

Implicit premise needed: ¬C excuses the Clause 1 obligation

Logical assessment: The argument is valid only if the implicit premise holds — that customs failure excuses performance. From P1+P2, ¬D follows (biconditional modus tollens). But this only establishes why delivery didn't occur, not that the supplier is excused from the Clause 3 penalty. Clause 3 simply says: if ¬D, then penalty. It does not contain a customs exception.

Buyer's strongest counter: Clause 3 is unconditional — ¬D → penalty, full stop. The reason for non-delivery is irrelevant to the penalty clause as drafted. If the supplier wanted a customs force majeure exception, it should have been written into the contract. The supplier is arguing for an implicit exception that the text does not contain — importing external reasoning into a formally closed conditional.

Multi-clause argument reconstruction · implicit premise identification · unconditional vs. conditional penalty clauses · the limits of extra-textual reasoning in contract interpretation
T2 · DThe contract states: "Payment of the penalty clause is due if and only if delivery is late AND the buyer provides written notice within 5 days." Buyer Z delivered notice on day 6. Seller claims the clause is now void entirely. Is the seller correct? Identify the logical structure and determine the exact legal consequence.

Formalise: P(clause triggers) ↔ [Late(delivery) ∧ Notice5(buyer)]. Buyer Z: Late=T (late delivery), Notice5=F (notice on day 6, not within 5 days). So Late∧Notice5 = T∧F = F → clause doesn't trigger.

The seller's claim that the clause is "void entirely" is incorrect. The clause is not void — it simply doesn't apply to this instance. It would apply in a future delivery that is both late AND has notice within 5 days. The biconditional means both conditions must hold simultaneously for the penalty to trigger; one missing condition leaves the clause dormant, not eliminated.

The distinction matters: void = no longer part of the contract; not triggered = valid but conditions not met. Sellers often conflate these to avoid future liability under the same clause.

Biconditional clause evaluation · conjunction-as-trigger · dormant vs void clause · one missed condition leaves the clause intact for future invocation
T2 · EA rescission clause reads: "Either party may rescind if performance is impossible OR if a material term has been breached." Buyer Q claims rescission because delivery of the specific model specified in the contract is now commercially unavailable. Seller argues Q cannot rescind because "impossibility requires absolute impossibility, not mere commercial inconvenience." Whose argument is stronger logically, and what does the logical structure tell you about how the dispute should be framed?

The clause: Rescind ← [Impossible(perf) ∨ Breach(material)]. A disjunction — either ground alone is sufficient. If Q establishes Impossible=T, rescission follows. If Q establishes Breach=T, rescission also follows.

The seller's argument attacks the DEFINITION of "impossible" (arguing it means absolute impossibility, not commercial unavailability). This is a definitional dispute about the predicate, not a structural challenge to the clause. Logically, whether Q can rescind depends entirely on how "impossible" is defined. If commercial unavailability = impossible (under the applicable law or contractual definition), Q wins. If impossible requires absolute impossibility, Q must rely on the breach ground instead.

The logical structure tells you this is a definitional/interpretive dispute, not a logical one. The form of the argument is valid; the fight is over the predicate's content. Q should argue BOTH grounds in the alternative — proving either one suffices.

Disjunctive rescission trigger · definitional dispute about a predicate · arguing in the alternative as a logical strategy · the fight is about predicate content, not logical structure
T3 · DFull integrated analysis: "If the goods conform to the specification, the buyer must accept them. If the buyer accepts the goods, the warranty period begins. The goods conform to specification except for a minor colour variation not mentioned in the contract. The buyer has not accepted the goods. Therefore no warranty period has begun, AND the buyer's non-acceptance is improper." Identify the argument type, formalise the key inferences, test validity of each conclusion, identify any suppressed premise, and evaluate whether both conclusions can stand simultaneously.

Formalisation: P1: C(x)→A(x) [conform→must accept]. P2: A(x)→W(x) [accept→warranty begins]. P3: C(goods) = TRUE with a minor caveat (colour variation). P4: A(goods) = FALSE (not accepted). C1: ¬W(goods) [no warranty]. C2: ¬A is improper [non-acceptance is wrongful].

C1 validity: From P2, A→W. Since ¬A, modus tollens gives... nothing actually. A→W and ¬A does not give ¬W. This is denying the antecedent. C1 is INVALID — warranty could have begun through some other path (e.g. deemed acceptance by conduct). To establish ¬W, you'd need ∀x[W(x)→A(x)] (warranty begins ONLY IF acceptance), which is not stated.

C2 validity: P1 says C→A. P3 says C(goods)=T. So by modus ponens, A(goods) is required (buyer must accept). But P4 says ¬A. C2 ("non-acceptance is improper") follows from P1+P3 — buyer is under a duty to accept and hasn't. C2 is VALID.

Can both stand simultaneously? C2 is valid; C1 is not. They can't both be sound because C1 rests on a logical error. More interestingly, if C1 were valid, the situation would be: buyer's non-acceptance is improper (C2) yet the warranty hasn't begun (C1). This is internally consistent — an improper act doesn't legally change the warranty trigger mechanism. But since C1 is invalid, the seller can't rely on it.

Suppressed premise in C1: to make C1 valid, you'd need: "Warranty begins ONLY IF the buyer accepts" (A→W, and W requires A). This converts the one-directional P2 into a biconditional W↔A, making ¬A → ¬W valid.

Denying the antecedent identified in C1 · C2 valid by modus ponens · two conclusions with different validity statuses · suppressed biconditional premise needed to rescue C1 · improper non-acceptance and warranty status as logically independent
T3 · EFull scenario analysis: Both parties' lawyers have submitted their opening positions. Lawyer A (seller): "All deliveries made within the contractual period are timely, and all timely deliveries discharge the seller's obligation. This delivery was made within the period, so the obligation is discharged. The buyer's penalty claim is therefore groundless." Lawyer B (buyer): "The delivery was within the contractual period but the goods were non-conforming. Non-conforming delivery is a breach regardless of timing. The discharge argument assumes conformity which is not established." (a) Identify the named inference pattern in Lawyer A's argument. (b) Identify the logical gap in Lawyer A's argument. (c) Evaluate Lawyer B's counter-argument structurally. (d) Whose argument is logically sounder, and what would Lawyer A need to add to close the gap?

(a) Lawyer A's pattern: P1: ∀x[Timely(x)→Discharge(x)]. P2: ∀x[InPeriod(x)→Timely(x)]. P3: InPeriod(delivery)=T. Chain: P3+P2→Timely(delivery); P1+Timely→Discharge. This is a valid hypothetical syllogism chain: two universals + specific instantiation → conclusion via modus ponens.

(b) Gap in Lawyer A's argument: The chain produces Discharge(delivery) — but only under the premise that a timely delivery is one made within the contractual period (P2). This premise DOES NOT include a conformity requirement. Lawyer A is implicitly assuming that "delivery" in P1 means "conforming delivery" — but that definition is unstated. If the duty of performance is to deliver conforming goods within the period, then InPeriod alone doesn't establish Timely(x) — Conforming(x)∧InPeriod(x)→Timely(x) would be the correct P2. Without conformity, the whole chain rests on an incomplete definition of "timely delivery."

(c) Lawyer B's counter-argument: B introduces a parallel rule: Non-conforming delivery → breach, independently of timing. This is ∀x[¬Conform(x)→Breach(x)]. Since ¬Conform(delivery)=T (goods were non-conforming), B concludes Breach=T. B also correctly diagnoses A's gap: A's argument "assumes conformity which is not established" — pointing directly at the missing predicate in A's P2.

(d) Sounder argument: Lawyer B's is structurally sounder because it targets the missing conjunct in A's definition. A's chain is valid given its premises, but those premises are incomplete. B is valid AND identifies the missing premise. To close the gap, Lawyer A would need to argue either: (i) the contract defines "conforming delivery" as including goods meeting specifications "within acceptable commercial tolerance" and this colour variation qualifies; or (ii) the penalty clause is only triggered by late delivery, not by non-conformity — making it a separate clause with a separate trigger. Argument (ii) requires reading the penalty clause in isolation from the general delivery standard.

Hypothetical syllogism chain · missing conjunct in a predicate definition · parallel breach rule as a counter-argument · diagnosing a gap in an opponent's premises · what Lawyer A needs to add to close the gap
SCENARIO 2
The Evidence Chain
Scenario
Facts

In a fraud trial, the prosecution presents the following chain of reasoning:

"All persons who signed the document knew its contents. All persons who knew its contents and signed were complicit. Person A signed the document. Therefore Person A was complicit."

Defence counsel argues: "My client signed under instructions and did not read the document. Furthermore, some signatories were told it was a routine form."

Questions
T2 Reconstruct the prosecution's argument in formal notation and identify the inference pattern used.
P1: ∀x, signed(x) → knew(x) [all signatories knew contents]
P2: ∀x, knew(x) ∧ signed(x) → complicit(x) [knowing signatories are complicit]
P3: signed(A) = T [Person A signed]

From P1 + P3 (Modus Ponens): knew(A) = T
From P2 + knew(A) + signed(A) (Modus Ponens): complicit(A) = T ✓

Pattern: Hypothetical syllogism across two universals, then modus ponens on the specific instance. Valid if all premises are true.

Multi-premise argument formalisation · hypothetical syllogism · universal instantiation
T2 Defence says some signatories were told it was a routine form. Which premise does this attack and how? What logical tool does this represent?

This attacks P1: "All persons who signed knew its contents."

The defence is producing the negation of P1: ∃x, signed(x) ∧ ¬knew(x) — there exists at least one signatory who did not know the contents (because they were told it was a routine form). One such person is sufficient to defeat the universal P1.

Logical tool: Negation of a universal statement — providing a counterexample (∃x ¬P(x)) to destroy a ∀ claim. Once P1 is defeated, the chain from signing to knowledge to complicity breaks at the first link.

Counterexample as ∀ negation · identifying which premise an argument attacks · chain argument vulnerability
T3 The prosecution responds: "Even if some signatories didn't know, Person A specifically was briefed on the document." Analyse how this repairs the prosecution's argument and whether the repaired argument is stronger or weaker than the original. What fallacy, if any, did the original argument risk?

How the repair works: The prosecution drops the universal P1 and replaces it with a specific factual assertion: knew(A) = T (established by direct evidence of briefing). The argument now runs:

P1 (revised): knew(A) = T [specific to Person A, not universal]
P2: knew(A) ∧ signed(A) → complicit(A)
P3: signed(A) = T
∴ complicit(A) = T ✓

Is the repaired argument stronger or weaker? Paradoxically, it is stronger. The original universal P1 was vulnerable to any single counterexample. The revised argument abandons the universal claim entirely and rests on a specific factual finding about Person A — which cannot be defeated by showing that other signatories did not know. The defence's counterexample no longer applies because the argument no longer depends on the universal.

Fallacy risk in the original: The original argument committed to a sweeping universal (all signatories knew) where only a specific claim was needed (this signatory knew). This is an example of over-generalisation — arguing at a broader level than the conclusion requires, which creates unnecessary vulnerability. The repaired argument is more surgically targeted and therefore more logically robust.

Argument repair · universal to specific narrowing · why a narrower premise is sometimes stronger than a universal · over-generalisation as a strategic error · the difference between logical validity and argumentative robustness
T2 · DThe prosecution argues: "CCTV placed the defendant near the scene. The defendant's phone pinged a tower within 400 metres. The defendant's fingerprints were on the door handle. No other person had access that night. Therefore the defendant committed the offence." Identify the argument type, name the logical structure, and identify the weakest link in the chain.

Argument type: Abductive (inference to the best explanation) structured as a cumulative inductive case. Each piece of evidence (CCTV, phone, fingerprints, access) is consistent with guilt; together they are presented as compellingly sufficient.

Logical structure: E1∧E2∧E3∧E4 → C. Four evidential conjuncts used to support a single conclusion. The argument is not deductively valid — none of the four premises individually or jointly entails guilt. It is inductively strong if the premises are true and alternative explanations are excluded.

Weakest link: "No other person had access that night." This is an exclusionary claim that is extremely difficult to establish with certainty — it asserts a universal negative about access. If even one alternative person had theoretical access, the entire argument loses its exclusionary force. The other three pieces of evidence are circumstantial; they confirm presence and contact, not commission of the offence. The CCTV, phone ping, and fingerprint evidence all have innocent explanations. Only the access-exclusion claim attempts to close off those alternatives — and it is the most empirically fragile premise.

Cumulative circumstantial evidence · abductive structure · weakest link as exclusionary negative claim · distinction between presence evidence and commission evidence
T2 · EDefence argues: "The CCTV image is inconclusive. Fingerprints on a public door handle prove nothing about entry at the relevant time. The phone-tower ping has a 400-metre radius — 12 other buildings are within that radius. Therefore reasonable doubt exists." Is this a logically complete argument for acquittal? What is it doing logically, and what is it not doing?

What it is doing logically: attacking each prosecution premise individually to show each is insufficient alone — weakening the evidential conjuncts. CCTV = inconclusive (E1 weaker). Fingerprints = non-probative for timing (E2 weaker). Phone ping = insufficient geolocation (E3 weaker). If each E is weakened, the conjunction E1∧E2∧E3 is weaker than each individually.

What it is NOT doing: (1) It is not establishing an alternative explanation. The defence shows the prosecution's evidence is weaker than presented, but doesn't explain who actually committed the offence or why the defendant was near the scene at that time. (2) It is not addressing the access claim (E4) — that "no other person had access" is left uncontested, which is the prosecution's strongest premise. Winning on E1-E3 while conceding E4 leaves a significant gap in the defence. (3) It doesn't establish an alibi or positive counter-narrative.

Logically complete? For reasonable doubt, it may be sufficient — doubt doesn't require an alternative explanation. But it's a purely defensive/negative strategy. A complete argument would add an alternative explanation for the presence evidence AND directly challenge the access claim.

Attacking evidential conjuncts · what reasonable doubt requires vs positive alternative narrative · uncontested E4 as a gap · negative vs affirmative defence strategy
T3 · DThe judge rules: "The prosecution has established that the defendant had opportunity and means. The defendant has not established any alibi. Therefore on the balance of probabilities, the defendant is liable." (This is a civil proceeding.) (a) Identify the logical structure of the ruling. (b) Identify a suppressed premise the ruling relies on. (c) The defence argues: "Absence of alibi is not the same as proof of presence." Evaluate this argument against the ruling's logic. (d) What does the ruling need to establish that it currently does not?

(a) Structure: P1: Defendant had opportunity and means (O∧M). P2: Defendant has no alibi (¬A). C: Defendant is liable on balance of probabilities. Form: (O∧M)∧¬A → Liable.

(b) Suppressed premise: "If a person had opportunity and means and cannot provide an alibi, they are more likely than not to be the person responsible." This is the key premise that makes the inference from opportunity+means+no-alibi to liability work. Without it, the argument is structurally invalid. The judge is implicitly treating O∧M∧¬A as sufficient for the civil standard — a disputed normative/evidentiary premise.

(c) Defence argument: Correct and significant. ¬Alibi means the defendant has not provided evidence of being elsewhere. It is logically distinct from Presence(defendant)=T. The ruling conflates two different statements: "defendant didn't prove absence" and "defendant was present." These are NOT equivalent. In formal terms, ¬A (no alibi established) ≠ ¬¬Present = Present. The judge would need to establish Presence independently, not infer it from the absence of its negation.

(d) What the ruling needs: positive evidence that the defendant was the specific person who committed the act — not just opportunity, means, and absence of alibi. On the balance of probabilities, the judge must find that it is more likely than not that THIS defendant did THIS act. That requires affirmative evidence of commission, not just the failure to disprove presence. The ruling in its current form rests on a burden-shifting that is not established: it treats the absence of an alibi as shifting the evidential burden to the defendant to disprove liability, which is not the civil standard.

Suppressed burden-shifting premise · no alibi ≠ proof of presence · what "balance of probabilities" actually requires in terms of affirmative evidence · burden of proof as a logical rather than purely procedural concept
T3 · ETwo expert witnesses give conflicting testimony. Expert W1: "DNA analysis shows a 1-in-50,000 chance that the DNA belongs to someone other than the defendant." Expert W2: "The sample was degraded and the matching protocol has a documented 3% false positive rate in samples of this type." The jury must decide. (a) Identify the logical structure of each expert's claim. (b) Are these claims contradictory, or can both be true simultaneously? (c) A juror argues: "1-in-50,000 proves guilt beyond reasonable doubt." Evaluate this inference. (d) How should a legally trained reasoner synthesise these two expert positions?

(a) Structure of each claim: W1: P(DNA belongs to another person | this sample) = 1/50,000. This is a probabilistic claim about population frequency — how rare a match this rare would be by chance in the general population. W2: P(false positive | degraded sample, this protocol) = 3%. This is a claim about the PROCESS reliability in this specific type of case, not population frequency.

(b) Contradictory or compatible? Compatible — both can be true simultaneously. They are measuring different things. W1's 1-in-50,000 is the random match probability (population statistic). W2's 3% is the protocol false positive rate (procedural statistic). In fact, if the protocol has a 3% false positive rate on degraded samples, then even if the underlying population match probability is 1-in-50,000, the practical probability of a false positive in THIS case is dominated by the 3% procedural error rate, not the 1-in-50,000 population figure. A 3% false positive rate means in 100 tests of this type, 3 will wrongly match — far higher than 1/50,000.

(c) Juror's inference: The "1-in-50,000 proves guilt" reasoning commits the Prosecutor's Fallacy. P(DNA from another person) = 1/50,000 is NOT the same as P(defendant is innocent | this DNA match) = 1/50,000. The second probability depends on many other factors including: base rate of guilt, quality of other evidence, and — critically — the protocol false positive rate. If W2 is correct about the 3% false positive rate on degraded samples, then the relevant probability for the juror is closer to 3% (false positive) than 1/50,000 (random population match). The 1-in-50,000 figure is valid only for an ideal, non-degraded sample using a reliable protocol.

(d) Synthesis: A legally trained reasoner should: (1) recognise that W1 and W2 are addressing different levels of analysis (population vs protocol); (2) understand that W2's concern, if factually established, overrides the significance of W1's figure for this specific evidence; (3) assess whether the degradation and protocol concerns are verified — if W2's 3% figure is established on record, it should substantially reduce the weight placed on the DNA match; (4) treat the DNA evidence as one element in a larger evidential matrix, weighted appropriately given the protocol concerns. Reasonable doubt is a qualitative standard; two competing expert positions on evidence reliability is a paradigm case where that standard should be carefully applied.

Population match probability vs protocol false positive rate · Prosecutor's Fallacy · two expert claims are compatible · how protocol reliability overrides population statistics for specific degraded samples · synthesis of conflicting expert positions
SCENARIO 3
The Regulatory Gap
Scenario
Facts

An environmental regulation states: "Any facility that discharges more than 50 litres of effluent per day into a waterway must obtain a permit."

Facility X discharges 45 litres per day directly into a river. No permit was obtained.

An inspector argues: "This facility is discharging into a waterway without a permit — it must be in violation." The facility's lawyer responds: "The regulation does not apply to us."

Questions
T2 Formalise the regulation as a universal conditional and determine whether the regulation applies to Facility X on these facts.
Let D(x) = "x discharges >50L/day into a waterway"
Let P(x) = "x must obtain a permit"
Regulation: ∀x, D(x) → P(x)

Facility X: discharges 45L/day → D(X) = F
Antecedent is false → conditional vacuously true
But: P(X) is NOT triggered — no permit required

The regulation does not apply. The facility's lawyer is correct. 45 litres is below the 50-litre threshold, so the antecedent is not satisfied and the obligation to obtain a permit is never triggered.

Universal conditional with a numerical threshold · vacuous truth when antecedent fails · threshold-based regulatory application
T2 The inspector's argument contains a logical flaw. Identify it precisely and name the fallacy.

Fallacy: Affirming the Consequent. The inspector reasons: the facility discharges into a waterway (partial q), has no permit (¬P), therefore violation. But this confuses the structure of the regulation.

More precisely, the inspector dropped the quantitative condition (>50L) from the antecedent — treating "discharging into a waterway" as if it alone triggered the permit requirement. The full antecedent is D(x) = ">50L/day AND into a waterway." By stripping the threshold, the inspector constructed a different, broader rule and applied it as if it were the actual regulation.

Antecedent truncation · affirming a modified consequent · the importance of reading all conditions of a legal rule precisely
T3 An environmental advocate argues: "The 50-litre threshold is arbitrary. Any discharge into a waterway should require a permit. The facility is exploiting a loophole." Evaluate this argument logically and legally. Is the facility exploiting a loophole, or operating within the law? What is the difference?

Logically: The advocate is arguing for a different regulation — one with no threshold (∀x, discharges(x) → permit(x)) — rather than challenging the facility's compliance with the existing one. This is a policy argument, not a legal compliance argument. The advocate is conflating "the law should be different" with "the facility has violated the existing law."

Legally: The facility is operating within the law as written. Compliance with the literal terms of a regulation is not a loophole — it is what compliance means. A loophole typically refers to a situation where the letter of the law is followed while its purpose is circumvented through some creative interpretation. Here, the facility is simply below the statutory threshold.

The difference: A loophole involves arguable tension between the text and the purpose. Operating below a clearly stated numerical threshold does not involve such tension — the legislature or regulator drew the line at 50 litres, and 45 litres is on the compliant side of that line. Whether 45 litres is environmentally harmful is a policy question that might justify amending the regulation — but it does not constitute non-compliance with the existing one.

Bar connection: This distinction — compliance vs. loophole exploitation — is central to tax law, regulatory law, and statutory interpretation. The advocate's argument is the structure of a legislative reform proposal, not a legal claim.

Policy argument vs. legal compliance argument · the definition of a loophole · text vs. purpose in statutory interpretation · the limits of logic in normative legal arguments
T2 · DA regulator's interpretation of the statute: "The Act covers 'commercial food preparation.' Street vendors were not mentioned in the drafting history. Therefore street vendors are not covered." Identify the logical structure and name the interpretive inference pattern being used. Then identify the counter-argument structure a street vendor's lawyer would use.

Regulator's structure: P1: The Act covers "commercial food preparation." P2: Street vendors were not mentioned in the drafting history. C: Street vendors are not covered. This is an Argument from Legislative Silence — the absence of a mention in drafting history is used as evidence of exclusionary intent. Logically, this is an Appeal to Ignorance (absence of evidence used as evidence of absence). The pattern: ¬Mentioned(street vendors) → ¬Covered(street vendors).

Interpretive pattern: Intentionalist/purposive exclusion — inferring that what was not explicitly included was intentionally excluded. This is valid only if the statute uses exhaustive enumeration. If the statute defines a general category ("commercial food preparation"), absence from drafting history is weak evidence of exclusion — it could equally indicate that street vendors were simply not thought of, or were assumed to fall within the general definition.

Counter-argument: Street vendors ARE commercial food preparation — they prepare food for commercial sale. The question is whether they satisfy the statutory definition's criteria, not whether they were mentioned. A literal/textual argument: street vendors satisfy "commercial" (for sale) + "food preparation" (they prepare food) → covered by plain meaning. The regulator's argument requires reading in an implicit exception ("unless not mentioned in drafting history") that doesn't appear in the text.

Argument from silence · absence of mention ≠ exclusion · exhaustive enumeration vs general category · literal vs purposive reading as competing logical frameworks
T2 · EThe statute says "No person shall operate a food business without a licence." A food blogger who receives no money but accepts free meals in exchange for reviews argues they are not operating a "food business" because they receive no cash payment. Formalise the blogger's argument. Is it logically valid? Identify the definitional pivot the argument depends on.

Formalisation: ∀x [FoodBusiness(x) → RequiresLicence(x)]. The blogger argues: ¬Cash(blogger) → ¬FoodBusiness(blogger) → ¬RequiresLicence(blogger). Premise: "Food business requires cash payment." Conclusion: since no cash, not a food business, therefore not covered.

Validity: Structurally, the blogger's argument is valid IF its definitional premise is accepted: FoodBusiness(x) ↔ Cash(x). But this is the entire dispute — whether "food business" means only cash-payment operations. If "food business" means "commercial activity involving food" and free meals in exchange for reviews is commercial (barter/in-kind payment), then FoodBusiness(blogger)=T and the licence is required.

Definitional pivot: The argument depends entirely on defining "food business" to exclude barter/in-kind exchanges. In-kind compensation (free meals = payment in the form of access to food) may satisfy the commercial element depending on the applicable legal definition of "commercial." The regulator would argue: free meals received in exchange for services ARE commercial consideration — the exchange is bilateral and economic even without cash. The blogger's argument conflates "no cash" with "no commercial activity," which conflates the means of payment (cash) with the nature of the transaction (commercial).

Predicate definition as the hinge point · cash vs in-kind consideration · barter as commercial activity · the regulator's definitional counter-argument · valid argument structure with contested predicate
T3 · DFull analysis: A food safety regulation requires "any business selling prepared food to the public" to display calorie information. Three businesses challenge the requirement: (A) a catering company that only serves private events (not "to the public"); (B) a restaurant that serves food but says the regulation doesn't specify which foods must display calories; (C) a food truck that operates on a weekly rotation — it argues that weekly operation doesn't meet the implied "regular" threshold implied by "business." Evaluate each challenge logically — identify which are definitional disputes, which attack the scope of the universal quantifier, and which challenge a suppressed premise.

Rule: ∀x [Sells(x, PrepFood, Public) → Display(x, CalorieInfo)].

Challenge A (catering company): Definitional dispute over "public." The catering company argues Private(events) → ¬Public → Sells(A, PrepFood, Public)=F → rule doesn't apply. This attacks the predicate "Public" — claiming private events are excluded by definition. Counter: "public" may mean "members of the public" (i.e. identifiable individuals, not a self-selected group), not "open to all." This is a definitional dispute about the scope of "Public."

Challenge B (restaurant): Attacks the scope of the CONSEQUENT — "which foods" must display calories. The rule says Display(CalorieInfo) but doesn't specify every item on the menu. B argues the universal "any business" triggers the requirement, but the WHAT to display is underspecified, creating a gap in implementation. This is a suppressed-premise challenge: the regulation suppresses the premise "every item on every menu must display calories." T3 · DA trial court found: "All of the defendant's witnesses gave consistent testimony. The prosecution's witnesses gave inconsistent testimony. Therefore the defendant's account is more credible." The inference is: Consistent(defendant's witnesses) ∧ ¬Consistent(prosecution's witnesses) → MoreCredible(defendant's account). This is invalid because consistency is a necessary but not sufficient condition for credibility.

Counterexample: A group of witnesses who have agreed on a false story in advance will be perfectly consistent — internal consistency is the hallmark of a well-prepared false narrative, not just of truth. Inconsistent prosecution witnesses might be independently telling the truth from different vantage points, with the inconsistencies reflecting genuine perceptual differences.

What consistency establishes: that the witnesses' accounts do not contradict each other — they could all be true simultaneously. Consistency is a logical prerequisite for credibility (contradictory testimonies cannot all be true) but is not sufficient for credibility. It tells you the accounts are co-possible, not that they are accurate.

What consistency does not establish: that any of the accounts are true; that the witnesses are independent (vs coordinated); that the accounts are accurate as opposed to agreed-upon. What the court SHOULD assess: whether the consistency is suspicious (too uniform, suggesting coordination) or natural (consistent on major facts, with innocent variation on minor details); whether the prosecution's inconsistencies are on material facts or peripheral details; whether any independent evidence corroborates either set of witnesses.

Person B is not challenging the rule (P1) or their status as an employer (P2) — only the factual premise that they failed to provide training.

Formalisation · identifying which factual premise is disputed · the difference between attacking the rule and attacking the facts
T2 The regulator says "no employees received adequate training." Person B says this is not the same as "failed to provide training." Is Person B making a valid logical distinction? What type of reasoning does this represent?

Person B is making a valid logical distinction.

failed_to_provide(x) ≠ provided_inadequate(x)

These are different predicates with different truth conditions.
One can be false while the other is true.

Person B is arguing that the predicate in the statute (failed_to_provide) was not satisfied, even if a different predicate (provided_inadequately) was. This is the same logic as antecedent satisfaction analysis — the regulator's finding satisfies a different antecedent from the one in the statute.

Type of reasoning: This is predicate logic applied to statutory interpretation — specifically, the argument that the statutory language must be read literally and that a different state of affairs from the one described in the statute does not trigger the statute's consequence.

T3 Person A responds: "Providing inadequate training is functionally equivalent to failing to provide training, because the purpose of the statute is worker safety, not box-ticking." Evaluate this argument. Does purpose-based interpretation change the logical structure of the statute? What is the deepest logical tension in this entire dispute?

Person A's argument: Purposive interpretation — the statute should be read in light of its objective (worker safety), not just its literal text. This means "failed to provide training" should be interpreted as "failed to provide training that achieves the statutory purpose," which includes inadequate training.

Does this change the logical structure? Yes. Person A is proposing to redefine the predicate failed_to_provide(x) to mean failed_to_provide_adequate(x). If this definitional expansion is accepted, Person B's distinction collapses — the predicates become equivalent by interpretive fiat rather than literal meaning.

Literal reading: failed_to_provide(B) = F → P3 false → no liability
Purposive reading: failed_to_provide(B) = T → P3 true → liability

The dispute is fundamentally about which reading of the predicate governs.

The deepest logical tension: The entire dispute reduces to a disagreement about the domain of the predicate "failed to provide training." Person B argues for a narrow, literal domain (training occurred or did not). Person A argues for a purposive domain (training achieved its statutory goal or did not). Logic cannot resolve this tension — it can only make it visible. The resolution requires a jurisprudential choice: does this court apply literal or purposive statutory interpretation?

This is the deepest truth about logic in law: formal logic clarifies the structure of arguments with crystalline precision, but it cannot determine which premises to accept when the premises themselves are contested. The judge must make a normative choice about the meaning of a predicate, and that choice is not a logical one.

T2 · DParty A claims: "The contract says 'all disputes shall be resolved by arbitration.' This is a dispute. Therefore arbitration is required." Party B argues: "The arbitration clause only applies to commercial disputes; this is a tortious claim, not a commercial dispute." Party C (the arbitrator) argues: "The question of whether this dispute falls within the clause is itself a dispute — so I have jurisdiction to decide my own jurisdiction." Identify the logical structure of each claim, and evaluate the circularity in Party C's argument.

Party A: ∀x [Dispute(x) → Arbitration(x)]. This(dispute)=T → Arbitration(this). Standard modus ponens on a universal. Valid if the universal applies without qualification.

Party B: The universal should be ∀x [CommercialDispute(x) → Arbitration(x)]. Since Tortious(this dispute) ∧ ¬Commercial(this dispute) → Arbitration(this) not established. This is a definitional challenge to the scope of the universal quantifier — Party B is arguing that "dispute" in the clause means "commercial dispute," not all disputes.

Party C (kompetenz-kompetenz argument): "Whether X is within my jurisdiction is itself a dispute → I have jurisdiction over jurisdiction-disputes → I can decide this." The circularity: Party C is using the existence of ANY dispute (including the jurisdictional dispute) to bootstrap jurisdiction. If Party C's rule were accepted, an arbitrator could claim jurisdiction over literally any dispute by characterising the jurisdictional question as itself a dispute. The argument is self-referential and potentially vicious — the term "dispute" in the arbitration clause cannot plausibly mean "any disagreement, including whether this clause applies to this disagreement." That reading makes the clause un-limitable.

Formally, Party C's argument requires the clause to mean ∀x [Dispute(x, whether-clause-applies) → Arbitration(x)], which is a different and far broader reading than "disputes arising under the contract." The kompetenz-kompetenz doctrine is real in arbitration law, but it doesn't give arbitrators unlimited bootstrapping authority — it gives them authority to make a preliminary ruling on jurisdiction, subject to court review. Party C's argument conflates a procedural power (preliminary determination) with a substantive power (final determination of own unlimited jurisdiction).

Modus ponens on universal clause · definitional dispute about scope of "dispute" · kompetenz-kompetenz as circular bootstrapping · scope of self-referential jurisdictional authority · difference between preliminary ruling power and unlimited jurisdiction
T2 · EThree parties signed the same master agreement but disagree about its interpretation. Party A says the agreement creates joint and several liability. Party B says it creates several liability only (each party liable for their own share). Party C says the agreement is silent on liability allocation and default rules apply. (a) What does it mean logically for the agreement to be "silent"? (b) Are any of the three interpretations contradictory? (c) Identify the logical step each party must take from their initial position to reach their claimed conclusion.

(a) What "silence" means logically: silence means the agreement contains no explicit proposition about liability allocation — the predicate Allocates(agreement, liability) is false, or more precisely, the agreement contains no rule of the form ∀party[Breach → Liability_type(party)]. Silence is an absence of a predicate, not a predicate with value false. It is an evidential gap in the text, not an affirmative claim about liability.

(b) Are any contradictory? A (joint and several) and B (several only) are CONTRADICTORY if applied to the same obligation — joint and several liability means each is liable for the whole; several means each is liable only for their part. These cannot both be true. C (default rules apply) is compatible with either A or B depending on what the default rules say — C is not making a substantive claim about the type of liability but about the SOURCE of the rule that governs. If default rules create joint and several, C and A are compatible; if default rules create several, C and B are compatible. C's position is interpretively prior to A and B.

(c) What each party needs:
A: must show that the agreement contains language that, properly interpreted, establishes joint liability (or that there is industry practice / default rule establishing it). The step: Text → JointAndSeveral via interpretation or implied term.
B: must show that the agreement expressly or impliedly allocates liability by share. Step: Text → SeveralOnly via explicit provision or implied exclusion of joint liability.
C: must show the text is genuinely silent (neither A nor B's reading is supported) and then invoke the applicable default rule. Step: ¬Text-covers-it → DefaultRuleApplies → DefaultRule→Liability_type.

Silence as absence of predicate · A and B contradictory, C interpretively prior · joint vs several as mutually exclusive · each party's inferential step from text to conclusion · default rules as gap-filling
T3 · DFull integrated scenario: A three-party commercial dispute involves these positions: Party A (supplier): "Clause 7 requires payment within 30 days of delivery. Delivery occurred 45 days ago. Therefore payment is overdue." Party B (buyer): "Clause 7 applies only to uncontested deliveries. We contested the delivery quality on day 3, so the 30-day clock never started." Party C (financier who has a security interest): "Regardless of A-B dispute, Clause 14 grants the financier first claim on any payments made. If B pays A, C must receive the funds first." Apply full integrated analysis: (a) identify the logical form of each position, (b) map all dependencies between the three positions, (c) identify which dispute must be resolved first for the others to make sense, and (d) evaluate whether all three positions can be simultaneously correct.

(a) Logical forms:
A: ∀x [Delivery(x) → PayWithin30(x)]. Delivery(this)=T, 45days>30days → PaymentOverdue(this). Modus ponens on universal.
B: ∀x [Delivery(x) ∧ Uncontested(x) → PayWithin30(x)]. Contested(this delivery, day3)=T → ¬Uncontested → PayWithin30 not triggered → no overdue payment.
C: Pays(B, A) → FirstClaim(C) ∧ FundsToC(first). Independent of A-B dispute — applies to any payment that occurs.

(b) Dependencies:
C's position is dependent on Pays(B, A)=T — if B never pays A (because B's position is upheld), C's first-claim right is triggered but there is nothing to claim. C's position is logically conditional on B paying.
A's position depends on the scope of Clause 7 — whether "delivery" in the universal quantifier is qualified by "uncontested" as B argues.
B's position is independent of C — B is challenging the trigger for ANY payment obligation, which logically precedes C's secondary allocation claim.
A and B are in direct conflict on the same clause; C's position is on a different clause and operates at a different level.

(c) Which dispute must be resolved first: The A-B dispute must be resolved first. If B's interpretation of Clause 7 is correct (contested delivery suspends the 30-day clock), then no payment is currently due, making C's Clause 14 right presently inoperative. Resolving the A-B dispute first determines whether ANY payment obligation exists. This is logically prior to C's priority claim because C's claim is contingent on a payment existing to prioritise. Courts would resolve: (1) Does Clause 7's trigger apply to contested deliveries? (2) If yes, is payment overdue? (3) If payment is made, does Clause 14 give C first claim? Steps 2 and 3 are sequentially dependent on Step 1.

(d) Can all three be simultaneously correct? Yes, they can all be correct simultaneously: A (payment is overdue under Clause 7), B (the 30-day clock was suspended by the contest — so EITHER B is wrong about suspension OR, if B is right, no payment is due), and C (if payment is made, C gets first claim). For all three to be simultaneously correct in a consistent world: the court must find that B's contest did NOT suspend the 30-day clock (A wins), payment IS overdue (A wins on the merits), AND C's Clause 14 applies to that overdue payment. In that scenario: A is correct that payment is overdue; B is wrong about suspension (their position fails); C is correct that they get first claim on any payment B makes. The three positions are not simultaneously correct in that sense — A and B's core claims are contradictory (payment triggered vs not triggered by contested delivery).

Three-party logical dependencies · which dispute is logically prior · conditional triggering of C's claim · A and B contradictory on Clause 7 scope · C's position as independent but contingent · sequential dependency in dispute resolution order
T3 · EA mediator summarises the three-party dispute: "All three parties agree the contract is binding. They disagree about what it means. But at bottom, their disagreement is not about the law — it's about the facts." A junior lawyer on Party A's team responds: "That's not right — even if the facts are agreed, the legal conclusion would still be disputed." Who is correct, and why? Identify what the mediator is getting wrong about the structure of a legal dispute, and what the junior lawyer is correctly identifying. Then explain the relationship between factual disputes, definitional disputes, and logical validity in multi-party contractual disputes of this type.

Who is correct: the junior lawyer. The mediator is wrong about the structure of the dispute.

What the mediator is getting wrong: the mediator is assuming that legal disputes reduce to factual disputes — that if you establish the facts, the legal conclusion follows automatically. This conflates "factual dispute" with "legal dispute." In this three-party case, even if EVERY factual question is agreed (delivery occurred on day X, contest was filed on day Y, payment hasn't been made), the legal dispute remains because the parties disagree about: (1) what "delivery" means in Clause 7 (definitional dispute); (2) whether a contest suspends the 30-day clock (interpretive dispute); (3) whether Clause 14's priority operates independent of the A-B dispute (legal-structural dispute). These are disputes about the PREDICATES and RULES, not about the facts the predicates are applied to.

What the junior lawyer correctly identifies: there is a logical level above the facts — the level at which predicates are defined, rules are interpreted, and logical relations between clauses are established. Even with agreed facts, you need agreed predicate definitions and agreed rule interpretations to reach agreed legal conclusions. The junior lawyer recognises that the dispute structure has at least three layers: facts → predicates/definitions → legal rules → conclusions. Agreement at the fact layer doesn't resolve disputes at the predicate/definition layer.

The relationship between factual disputes, definitional disputes, and logical validity: factual disputes are about whether a predicate is satisfied for a specific individual (Was delivery on day X? Did B contest on day Y?). Definitional disputes are about what the predicate means (Does "delivery" in Clause 7 include contested deliveries?). Logical validity is about whether the inference from premises to conclusion is correct given the predicates and rules. A legal argument can be logically valid (the inference is correct) while being UNSOUND because either a factual premise is false or a definitional premise is contested. In this case, even if the argument is logically valid under A's reading, it is not agreed to be sound because B contests the predicate definition. The mediator is conflating all three levels; the junior lawyer is correctly identifying that definitional disputes survive factual agreement.
