Deterministic vs. Probabilistic Models
Language First

A deterministic model gives one exact output for each input. Same input, same output, every time. No randomness. "Distance = speed × time" is deterministic — 60 km/h for 2 hours is always exactly 120 km.

A probabilistic model gives a range of possible outputs with likelihoods. "A person who smokes 20 cigarettes a day has a 30% probability of lung cancer by age 60." Same input, different possible outcomes — it produces a distribution, not a single answer.

Deterministic: y = f(x) — one x gives exactly one y
Probabilistic: P(Y|X=x) — given x, what is the probability distribution of Y?
The Translation Question PU Will Ask

"Every person exposed to the chemical develops the disease" — deterministic (absolute claim). "A person has a 60% chance of developing the disease" — probabilistic. Casual language blurs this. Detect which model the claim implies before answering.

Mechanics & Notation
Deterministic test: does the same input always produce the identical output, with zero variation? If yes, deterministic.
Probabilistic test: does the claim involve a rate, percentage, "typically," "tends to," or any language admitting exceptions? If yes, probabilistic.
Mixed claims: a deterministic rule can govern a probabilistic input — e.g. "if convicted (deterministic legal consequence), sentence length follows a probabilistic distribution based on prior record."

The keyword test is fast but not foolproof — "always" can appear in a probabilistic claim used loosely ("violent offenders are always considered higher risk"), so the real test is whether the claim, taken literally and precisely, logically admits exceptions. If it does, even one, it's probabilistic regardless of how confident the wording sounds.

Worked Examples
Easy — Two Clear Statements, One of Each Type

"Contracts signed under duress are voidable." / "Most contracts signed under duress are later challenged in court."

First: Deterministic. "Are voidable" is a fixed legal consequence — given the condition (duress), the outcome (voidable) follows with certainty under the rule. No probability involved; the output is the same for every input satisfying the condition.

Second: Probabilistic. "Most" is a statistical frequency claim — it describes what tends to happen across many cases, not what is guaranteed to happen in any specific one. A contract signed under duress might or might not be challenged; "most" simply says challenges are the more common outcome.

Medium — Classifying a Mixed Statutory Claim

"Any person who drives with a blood alcohol level above the legal limit is guilty of an offence. Studies show such drivers are involved in collisions at roughly 4 times the rate of sober drivers."

First sentence: deterministic. "Is guilty" admits no exceptions once the antecedent (above the limit) is satisfied — this is a legal rule, y = f(x) with one fixed output.

Second sentence: probabilistic. "Roughly 4 times the rate" is a statistical tendency drawn from a distribution of outcomes across many drivers — it says nothing certain about any individual driver.

A single paragraph can mix both model types, and the skill is classifying each claim independently rather than letting the confident tone of one infect your read of the other.

Hard — Ambiguous Passage, Four Statements, Three Model Types

"All food products containing allergen X must be labelled. [1] Mislabelled products cause consumer harm in roughly 1 in 800 cases. [2] If a consumer is harmed, the manufacturer is liable. [3] Manufacturers who have been found liable tend to improve labelling compliance within 12 months. [4]"

[1] Deterministic — legal rule, fixed consequence.

[2] Probabilistic — statistical frequency ("roughly 1 in 800"), not a guaranteed outcome.

[3] Deterministic — strict conditional legal rule (harm → liability), no probability.

[4] Probabilistic with causal ambiguity — "tend to" is a statistical tendency. Also note the correlation/causation trap: liability findings are correlated with later improvements, but the causal mechanism (shame, financial pressure, regulatory monitoring) is unstated. This is the most complex statement: probabilistic in structure AND subject to misinterpretation as a causal claim.

Why it is Hard: [4] requires identifying both the probabilistic structure AND the causal ambiguity simultaneously — a classifier who stops at "this is probabilistic" has given only a partial answer.

T3 · AA risk model predicts: "Facilities with safety scores below 60 have a 78% probability of a serious incident within 5 years." Facility X scores 55 and has had no incident. The regulator wants to close it. The facility argues: "Probability is not certainty." Analyse both positions using the deterministic/probabilistic distinction.

The facility is logically correct but strategically weak. The model is explicitly probabilistic — 0.78, not 1.0. No incident to date is consistent with being in the 22% that don't experience incidents. The facility correctly notes probability is not certainty.

T2 · DA regulatory threshold states: "A factory emitting more than 50 ppm of compound Y must shut down immediately." An environmental report states: "Factories emitting above 40 ppm are associated with a 30% higher incidence of respiratory illness in nearby populations." Classify each statement and explain the difference in how a regulator would use each one in an enforcement action.

The regulatory threshold is deterministic: emit > 50 ppm → must shut down. The output is fixed; there is no probability of shutdown being required — it is required. A regulator uses this to issue a shutdown order without any further discretion once the threshold is confirmed exceeded.

The environmental report is probabilistic: a statistical association (30% higher incidence) drawn from population data. It describes a tendency across many factories and communities, not a guaranteed outcome for any specific factory or resident. T2 · EA contract clause reads: "If either party fails to perform within the agreed period, the non-performing party shall pay a penalty of Rs.10,000 per day of delay." A consultant's report states: "Based on historical project data, parties facing this type of penalty clause settle within 15 days on average." Classify both, then explain why a lawyer advising on contract risk cannot substitute the probabilistic statement for the deterministic one in their legal analysis.

Clause: Deterministic — delay → Rs.10,000/day. The penalty is fixed and follows automatically from the triggering condition; there is no range or probability in the legal obligation. Consultant report: Probabilistic — a statistical average over historical data. "On average" means 15 days is the central tendency; some settle in 5, some in 60.

A lawyer advising on risk cannot substitute the probabilistic statement for the deterministic one because: (1) the client's legal obligation under the clause is Rs.10,000/day regardless of what other parties historically do; (2) the client may not be a "typical" party — their specific dispute may resolve faster or slower than the average; (3) legal advice requires precision about what is owed, not statistical comfort about what usually happens. The consultant's data is relevant for negotiating strategy (how quickly to settle) not for establishing legal liability (what the clause requires). Substituting probabilistic averages for deterministic obligations is a category error that could expose a client to unexpected losses.

T3 · BA judge's sentencing guideline states: "Offenders convicted of a scheduled offence for the second time shall receive a sentence of not less than 3 years." [1] A criminological study states: "Second-time offenders sentenced under mandatory minimums reoffend at a rate 12% lower than those sentenced with judicial discretion." [2] The defence argues: "The study shows mandatory minimums are only modestly effective — the judge should exercise discretion." The prosecution argues: "The study shows mandatory minimums work — the guideline should be applied." (a) Classify both statements. (b) Identify the logical error in both lawyers' uses of statement [2]. (c) What does statement [2] actually establish, and what does it not?

(a) [1] Deterministic: second conviction → minimum 3 years, fixed by the rule. [2] Probabilistic: a comparative statistical claim (12% lower reoffending rate) drawn from population data across many cases.

(b) Both lawyers' errors: The defence uses the 12% to argue the EFFECT is too modest to justify mandatory application — this is a policy argument using a probabilistic statistic, but it ignores that the judge has no discretion under a mandatory minimum regardless of the statistic's size. The prosecution uses the 12% to argue the guideline "works" — but [2] doesn't establish causation, only correlation (the reoffending difference might be explained by offender characteristics rather than sentencing type). Both lawyers are making policy arguments from a probabilistic study and applying them to a deterministic rule, which is a category error.

(c) What [2] establishes and does not: Establishes: an observed statistical association between sentencing type and reoffending rate across a study population. Does not establish: causation (whether mandatory minimums cause the lower rate or select for different offender profiles); applicability to this specific defendant (who may differ from the study population); that the guideline should or shouldn't be applied (a normative conclusion requiring much more than a 12% correlation).

T3 · CA contract dispute involves two competing expert witnesses. Expert W1 (for plaintiff): "The industry standard for delivery turnaround is 14 days, and missing this standard constitutes a material breach." Expert W2 (for defendant): "Of all similar contracts in the last five years, 23% were delivered beyond 14 days without being treated as breaches by either party." The contract itself is silent on what constitutes a material breach. (a) Classify both expert claims. (b) Identify what each expert is actually providing. (c) Which expert's evidence is more directly relevant to the legal question of whether a material breach occurred, and why? (d) If the court accepts both as accurate, can it simultaneously find that a material breach occurred?

(a) W1: Quasi-deterministic — presents the 14-day standard as a fixed rule (breach = missing it). W2: Probabilistic — presents a statistical distribution of actual industry practice (23% delivered late without consequence).

(b) What each provides: W1 provides a normative industry standard — what the industry says should happen. W2 provides a descriptive empirical finding — what the industry actually does. These are fundamentally different types of evidence: one is a rule, the other is a frequency.

(c) More directly relevant evidence: W2's evidence is arguably more relevant to the question of "material breach" in a contract that is silent on the standard. "Material" in contract law often depends on whether the breach goes to the root of the contract or whether industry practice treats it as significant. W2's finding that 23% of late deliveries are accepted without being treated as breaches suggests the 14-day standard is NOT universally treated as material in practice. W1's evidence supports what the standard should be; W2's evidence shows what it is. For a court interpreting an ambiguous contract, descriptive industry practice often carries significant weight alongside normative standards.

(d) Both accurate — can material breach be found? Yes. Both can be accurate simultaneously without contradiction: the industry standard is 14 days (W1 is accurate) AND 23% of late deliveries aren't treated as material (W2 is accurate). A court could find material breach if it determines the standard applies (W1) while accepting that the 23% represents cases where parties chose to waive their rights rather than cases where no breach occurred. The key distinction: W2 shows that breach is sometimes tolerated, not that it doesn't occur. Tolerance of breach ≠ absence of breach.

Normative standard (quasi-deterministic) vs empirical frequency (probabilistic) · what the industry says vs what it does · materiality of breach as the legal question · both accurate without contradiction · waiving breach vs breach not occurring

Expert reports routinely present probabilistic findings using language that sounds deterministic — a lawyer who can't separate "this risk factor is associated with a 30% increase" from "this risk factor causes the outcome" will either overstate or understate the report's actual evidential weight in cross-examination.

LEC 12
Linear Functions & Real-World Modeling
Language First

A linear function describes a relationship where change is constant. For every additional unit of input, the output changes by the same fixed amount. The graph is a straight line.

y = mx + c
m = slope = rate of change (how much y changes per unit of x)
c = y-intercept = value of y when x = 0 (the starting point)

Slope from two points: m = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁)

In plain language: a lawyer who charges a fixed consultation fee plus an hourly rate has a linear total fee. Hourly rate = m. Fixed fee = c. Hours worked = x.

The Word Problem Translation Step

PU describes a situation in prose. First move: identify what is constant per period (slope m) and what is fixed regardless of x (intercept c). Never write an equation before you have identified both.

Mechanics & Notation
Building a model from prose, step by step:
1. Find the fixed amount (appears regardless of usage) → c
2. Find the per-unit rate (multiplied by usage) → m
3. Identify the input variable and what it counts → x
4. Assemble: y = mx + c

Solving for x given y: y = mx + c → x = (y − c) / m

Word problems frequently state the fixed and variable amounts in either order, or bury the fixed amount mid-sentence rather than at the start — the mechanical fix is to extract both numbers first, label them m and c explicitly, and only then write the equation, rather than trying to write the equation directly from the prose in one pass.

Worked Examples
Easy — Read Off the Model Directly

A law firm charges a fixed registration fee of Rs.2,000 plus Rs.500 per hour of work. Write the cost function and identify slope and intercept.

C(t) = 500t + 2000. Slope = 500 (Rs.500 added per hour), intercept = 2000 (fixed cost regardless of hours). For t=0 hours, C=Rs.2,000; for t=4 hours, C=Rs.4,000. Linear because each additional hour adds the same fixed amount.

Medium — Building the Model

"A law firm charges a fixed registration fee of Rs. 5,000 plus Rs. 2,000 per hour."

c = 5,000 (fixed). m = 2,000 (per hour). Model: Fee = 2,000x + 5,000.

At 3 hours: 2,000(3) + 5,000 = Rs. 11,000. For Rs. 15,000 budget: 15,000 = 2,000x + 5,000 → x = 5 hours.

Hard — Build, Verify, Apply, Identify Limits

A regulatory authority tracks complaints against a company: 12 complaints in January (month 1) and 36 complaints in month 4. Assuming a linear model, (a) build the model, (b) predict month 7 complaints, (c) determine when complaints reach 100, (d) identify one assumption that might break the model.

(a) Two points: (1,12) and (4,36). Slope m = (36−12)/(4−1) = 24/3 = 8 complaints/month. Intercept: 12 = 8(1) + b → b = 4. Model: C(t) = 8t + 4.

(b) Month 7: C(7) = 56 + 4 = 60 complaints.

(c) 8t + 4 = 100 → 8t = 96 → t = 12. Month 12.

(d) Assumptions that might break it: complaints can't be negative or fraction (the model has no lower bound constraint); companies typically respond to complaint spikes with remediation (growth might not be linear after month 6); seasonal effects might distort the trend. A regulator should treat month 12 as a projection, not a guarantee.

T3 · CAn expert witness in a wage dispute testifies: "Based on industry data, I modelled monthly earnings as E(t) = 1200t + 18000 where t is years of experience. Using this model, an employee with 8 years of experience who earns Rs.26,000/month is being underpaid by Rs.1,600." Defence counsel challenges: "The model was built from data with a range of 2–6 years experience. Applying it to 8 years is extrapolation." (a) Compute what the model predicts for t=8 and verify the expert's underpayment claim. (b) Explain the extrapolation concern as a modeling limitation. (c) How should a court weigh the expert's testimony given the extrapolation issue?

(a) E(8) = 1200(8) + 18000 = 9600 + 18000 = Rs.27,600. Actual salary: Rs.26,000. Underpayment: 27,600 − 26,000 = Rs.1,600. The expert's computation is correct given the model.

(b) Extrapolation concern: the model E(t) = 1200t + 18000 was fitted to data from t=2 to t=6 (a 4-year range). Applying it to t=8 assumes the linear relationship continues beyond the observed data — that each year beyond year 6 continues to add Rs.1,200/month. This may not be true: earnings growth often slows or plateaus at higher experience levels (a linear model may fit the mid-career range but flatten thereafter). The model's accuracy outside its calibration range is unknown; the linear trend could overestimate what experienced employees actually earn in the market, making the "underpayment" appear larger than it actually is.

(c) Court weighing: the court should accept the model as valid within its calibration range (2–6 years) but demand corroboration for t=8. Specifically: (i) Are there any data points near t=8 in the dataset? (ii) Is there theoretical or empirical reason to believe the linear trend extends to t=8 in this industry? (iii) Can the expert provide the confidence interval at t=8 (which will be wider than at t=4 because extrapolation increases uncertainty)? The Rs.1,600 underpayment should be presented as the model's extrapolated estimate with an identified uncertainty range, not as a precise factual finding. Defence counsel's extrapolation challenge is legitimate and should reduce — but not eliminate — the weight of the testimony.

LEC 12
Interpreting Slope & Intercept
Language First

Slope (m) answers: "How fast is this changing?" — the rate of change per unit of x.

Intercept (c) answers: "What is the baseline?" — the value when x is zero.

Positive slope: y increases as x increases — growth
Negative slope: y decreases as x increases — decay or reduction
Zero slope: y is constant — no relationship with x
Mechanics & Notation
Reading slope from prose: look for "per," "each," "every," "rate of" — the number attached to one of these words is m, and its sign comes from whether the quantity is rising or falling.
Reading intercept from prose: look for "starting," "initial," "at the outset," "before any [x] occurred" — this is the value at x=0.
Comparing two models: set them equal to find the crossover point; check which has the larger |slope| to determine which changes faster long-term, regardless of which starts higher.

A model with a small intercept but large slope will eventually overtake a model with a large intercept but small slope — the crossover point is exactly where m₁x + c₁ = m₂x + c₂, solved for x.

Worked Examples
Easy — Read and Interpret Slope and Intercept

Fine model: F(d) = 200d + 500, d = days of non-compliance, F = total fine in Rs. Slope (200): Each additional day adds Rs.200 to the fine — marginal penalty rate. Intercept (500): Rs.500 base fine regardless of duration, owed as soon as non-compliance is confirmed. F(0) = Rs.500. At d=10: F = Rs.2,500. The daily penalty overtakes the base fine after the first 2.5 days.

Medium — Interpreting and Comparing Two Models

"Compliance costs for Firm A start at Rs. 200,000 and rise by Rs. 40,000 per year. Compliance costs for Firm B start at Rs. 350,000 and rise by Rs. 15,000 per year."

Firm A: c = 200,000, m = 40,000 → A(t) = 40,000t + 200,000. Firm B: c = 350,000, m = 15,000 → B(t) = 15,000t + 350,000.

Crossover: 40,000t + 200,000 = 15,000t + 350,000 → 25,000t = 150,000 → t = 6 years.

Before year 6, Firm B has higher costs (larger intercept). After year 6, Firm A overtakes — its steeper slope eventually dominates the smaller starting gap. A firm choosing between the two structures needs to know its planning horizon, not just which number looks smaller today.

Hard — Two Competing Penalty Models, Advise the Client

Model X: F(d) = 150d + 1000. Model Y: F(d) = 250d + 200. A business expects 8 days of non-compliance. Which model produces higher penalties, and if the business could negotiate which regime applies, which should it prefer?

F_X(8) = 150(8) + 1000 = 1200 + 1000 = Rs.2,200. F_Y(8) = 250(8) + 200 = 2000 + 200 = Rs.2,200. At d=8, both models produce the same penalty. Crossover: 150d+1000 = 250d+200 to 800 = 100d to d=8. Identical at exactly 8 days.

For d < 8: F_Y < F_X (Model Y is cheaper). For d > 8: F_Y > F_X. Advice: if confident of resolution under 8 days, prefer Model Y. If risk of extended delay, prefer Model X (higher base, lower daily rate). The advice turns on the business confidence in its remediation timeline.

LEC 14
Systems of Linear Equations
Language First

A system of linear equations requires two or more conditions to be satisfied simultaneously. Geometrically: each equation is a line — the solution is where they cross.

One solution: lines cross at one point — consistent, independent
No solution: lines are parallel — inconsistent
Infinite solutions: lines are identical — dependent

Substitution: solve one equation for one variable, substitute
Elimination: multiply to match coefficients, then add/subtract
Translation Step — Always First

Define variables explicitly before writing equations. "Let x = ... and y = ..." written down forces the translation and prevents setup errors. Never skip this step.

Mechanics & Notation
Elimination procedure:
1. Multiply one or both equations so a variable's coefficients match (or are opposite)
2. Add (if opposite signs) or subtract (if same sign) to cancel that variable
3. Solve the resulting single-variable equation
4. Substitute back into either original equation to find the second variable
5. Check both original equations with both values

Substitution is faster when one equation already isolates a variable cleanly; elimination is faster when both equations are in standard form (ax + by = c) with no variable pre-isolated. Word problems rarely tell you which to use — recognising the equations' shape after translation is what determines the efficient method, not a rule to memorise in isolation.

Worked Examples
Easy — Direct Substitution, Two Variables

A law firm bills Partner A at Rs.4,000/hr and Associate B at Rs.2,000/hr. A case used 8 hours total and cost Rs.24,000. How many hours did each work?

System: a + b = 8; 4000a + 2000b = 24000. From first equation: b = 8 - a. Substitute: 4000a + 2000(8-a) = 24000 → 4000a + 16000 - 2000a = 24000 → 2000a = 8000 → a = 4. b = 4. Partner: 4 hours, Associate: 4 hours.

Medium — Solving by Elimination

"A settlement fund pays Party X and Party Y. Three times Party X's payment plus Party Y's payment totals Rs. 280,000. Party X's payment minus Party Y's payment is Rs. 40,000."

Let X = Party X payment, Y = Party Y payment
Eq.1: 3X + Y = 280,000
Eq.2: X − Y = 40,000

Add Eq.1 + Eq.2 (Y cancels): 4X = 320,000 → X = 80,000
Substitute into Eq.2: 80,000 − Y = 40,000 → Y = 40,000
Check Eq.1: 3(80,000) + 40,000 = 280,000 ✓

Party X receives Rs. 80,000, Party Y receives Rs. 40,000 — elimination was faster here than substitution because both equations were already in a form where adding them directly cancelled Y.

Hard — Three Variables, Resource Allocation, Legal Interpretation

A firm allocates 100 hours across drafting (d), research (r), and meetings (m). Research takes twice as long as meetings: r = 2m. Total billing (at Rs.3000d + Rs.2000r + Rs.1500m) = Rs.225,000.

System: d + r + m = 100; r = 2m; 3000d + 2000r + 1500m = 225000. Substitute r=2m into first: d + 2m + m = 100, d + 3m = 100, d = 100-3m. Into cost: 3000(100-3m) + 2000(2m) + 1500m = 225000. 300000 - 9000m + 4000m + 1500m = 225000. 300000 - 3500m = 225000. 3500m = 75000. m = 75000/3500 = 21.4 hours. r = 42.9 hours. d = 35.7 hours.

Interpretation: The firm spends 35.7% of time drafting (highest billing rate), 42.9% researching (mid rate), 21.4% in meetings (lowest rate). A managing partner reviewing this allocation might question whether meetings are being time-recorded accurately — 21 hours in meetings on a 100-hour case is high and the per-hour rate is lowest.

LEC 15
Linear Inequalities & Their Applications
Language First

An inequality describes a range of acceptable values. Legal thresholds, regulatory limits, and damage caps are inequalities in disguise.

Solving rule — the one that trips everyone:
When you multiply or divide both sides by a NEGATIVE number,
the inequality sign FLIPS direction.

2x > 10 → x > 5 (divided by +2, unchanged)
-2x > 10 → x < -5 (divided by -2, FLIPS)

Build the habit: every time you divide or multiply by a negative, explicitly pause, check, and flip.

Mechanics & Notation
Compound inequality: a ≤ x ≤ b means BOTH x ≥ a AND x ≤ b must hold simultaneously — never just one
Quadratic inequality procedure: 1. Move everything to one side (≤ 0 or ≥ 0 form) 2. Factor 3. Find the roots 4. Determine the sign of the parabola between/outside the roots based on whether it opens up or down
Sign chart shortcut: for (x−r₁)(x−r₂), the expression is negative between the roots and positive outside them, when the parabola opens upward

The single most exam-relevant fact about compound inequalities: a value satisfying only one half of a two-sided constraint has not satisfied the constraint at all — this is the same ∀ vs. ∃ distinction from Part 1, now showing up inside ordinary algebra rather than formal logic notation.

Worked Examples
Easy — Solve and Interpret a Simple Linear Inequality

A contractor must spend no more than Rs.50,000 on materials (m) and at least Rs.20,000 (to meet minimum quality standards). Express as a compound inequality, solve, and interpret.

Inequalities: m ≤ 50,000 AND m ≥ 20,000. Written as compound: 20,000 ≤ m ≤ 50,000. The feasible spending range is Rs.30,000 wide. Any material cost within this range is contractually permissible. Below Rs.20,000 violates the quality floor; above Rs.50,000 violates the budget cap.

Medium — Solving a Quadratic Inequality Step by Step

Solve: x² - 2x - 15 ≥ 0

Already in ≥ 0 form.
Factor: (x-5)(x+3) ≥ 0
Roots: x = 5, x = -3
Parabola opens upward (positive x² coefficient) → expression is POSITIVE outside the roots, negative between them
Since we want ≥ 0 (positive or zero): x ≤ -3 OR x ≥ 5

Check with a test point in each region: x=0 (between roots) → (0-5)(0+3) = -15, negative — correctly excluded. x=6 (outside, right) → (6-5)(6+3)=9, positive — correctly included. The test-point check is the fast way to confirm a sign chart without re-deriving the parabola's shape from scratch.

Hard — Compound Inequality with Legal Consequence at Each Boundary

An employment contract says an employee must work at least 40 hours/week and no more than 60 hours/week. Additional clauses: overtime pay kicks in at 48 hours (time-and-a-half), and a health-and-safety violation occurs above 55 hours.

Regime 1: 40 ≤ h ≤ 48. Standard pay, fully compliant.

Regime 2: 48 < h ≤ 55. Standard pay for first 48, overtime (1.5x) for (h-48) hours. No H&S violation.

Regime 3: 55 < h ≤ 60. Overtime applies AND H&S violation threshold exceeded — employer faces potential regulatory liability even though hours are within the contractual cap.

h > 60: breach of contract (exceeds cap) AND H&S violation. Both obligations violated simultaneously.

Legal insight: the contractual ceiling (60 hours) and the regulatory ceiling (55 hours for H&S) are different thresholds creating different legal consequences. An employee working 58 hours is within the contract but violating H&S — the employer can enforce the contract yet face regulatory sanction. Compound inequalities reveal this multi-regime structure that a flat "allowed hours" statement would obscure.

LEC 16
Introduction to Linear Programming
Language First

Linear programming answers: "Given constraints, what is the best possible outcome?" It optimises an objective function subject to inequality constraints.

1. Decision variables: what you are choosing (x, y)
2. Objective function: what to maximise or minimise
3. Constraints: the limitations (budget, capacity, regulatory limits)

The optimal solution always occurs at a corner point of the feasible region.

A regulator allocating inspection resources, a law firm deciding how to allocate associate time across cases, a company maximising output within environmental permit limits — all are linear programming problems.

Mechanics & Notation
Corner point method, step by step:
1. Graph every constraint inequality
2. Identify the feasible region — the area satisfying all constraints simultaneously
3. Find every corner (vertex) of that region by solving pairs of constraint equations together
4. Evaluate the objective function at every corner
5. The maximum (or minimum) always occurs at one of those corners — never in the interior, never on an edge's midpoint

The corner-point guarantee is what makes linear programming tractable by hand: instead of checking infinitely many points in the feasible region, you only ever need to check a small, finite list of vertices. Missing a corner is the single most common source of a wrong "optimal" answer — always count the corners systematically rather than eyeballing the graph for the best-looking point.

Worked Examples
Easy — Identify Objective and Constraints, No Computation

A law firm can handle two case types: contract disputes (C) and regulatory matters (R). Each contract case requires 20 partner hours and earns Rs.80,000 profit. Each regulatory matter requires 30 partner hours and earns Rs.100,000. The firm has 150 partner hours available per month and must handle at least 2 of each type.

Objective: Maximise P = 80,000C + 100,000R. Constraints: 20C + 30R ≤ 150 (hours); C ≥ 2; R ≥ 2; C, R ≥ 0. This is an LP setup — all constraints are linear, the objective is linear, and the decision variables are the number of each case type. No computation yet — the skill here is correctly identifying the objective function and expressing constraints in standard inequality form.

Medium — Full LP Setup and Corner Point Evaluation

A firm produces two products, P and Q. Each unit of P needs 2 hours labour and yields Rs. 30 profit. Each unit of Q needs 4 hours labour and yields Rs. 50 profit. Available labour: 40 hours. Maximum 15 units of P can be sold (market cap).

Maximise Z = 30P + 50Q
Subject to: 2P + 4Q ≤ 40  (labour)
        P ≤ 15  (market cap)
        P, Q ≥ 0

Finding the corners: (0,0): Z=0. (15,0): labour used = 30 ≤ 40, feasible, Z=450. (0,10): from 2(0)+4Q=40, Z=500. (15,2.5): intersection of P=15 and 2(15)+4Q=40 → Q=2.5, Z=30(15)+50(2.5)=575.

Optimal: P=15, Q=2.5, Z=575. Notice the highest-profit corner isn't the one using all labour on the highest per-unit-profit product (Q) alone — it's the intersection of two binding constraints together, which is exactly why checking every corner systematically matters more than reasoning informally about which product "seems" better.

Hard — Full LP with Non-Obvious Optimal Vertex

A compliance firm optimises audit and advisory services (A = audits, V = advisory). Profit: 60,000A + 45,000V. Constraints: 8A + 5V ≤ 80 (analyst hours); 3A + 6V ≤ 54 (partner review hours); A ≥ 0; V ≥ 0.

LEC 17
Linear vs. Exponential Growth — The Core Distinction
Language First — The Single Most Important Distinction in Part 3

Linear growth: you add the same amount each period. Like saving Rs. 1,000 every month — each month the total increases by a fixed amount.

Exponential growth: you multiply by the same factor each period. Like a bank account growing at 10% per year — each year the increase is larger than the last because you are earning interest on a bigger balance.

The difference in plain language: linear adds, exponential multiplies.

Linear: y = mt + c — add m every period
Exponential: y = a · bᵗ — multiply by b every period
where a = initial value, b = growth factor

Growth factor b:
b > 1 → growth (e.g. b=1.15 means 15% increase per period)
0 < b < 1 → decay (e.g. b=0.80 means 20% decrease per period)
The QR-I Killer — Now in QR-II

This is the exact category that produced the base-conversion question in QR-I. PU will give you a word problem and you must decide: does this situation add a fixed amount (linear) or multiply by a fixed factor (exponential)? Salary increase by Rs. 5,000/year — linear. Salary increase by 8% per year — exponential. Population growing by 200 people/year — linear. Population growing at 3% per year — exponential. The signal words: "by X per period" (linear) vs. "by X% per period" or "grows at a rate of" (exponential).

P = initial value · r = rate (as decimal) · t = time periods

Exponential decay: A = P(1 - r)ᵗ

Continuous growth (natural e): A = Peʳᵗ
Used when growth is continuous rather than periodic

Approximate years to double = 70 / (rate as percentage)

LEC 17
Exponential Equations & Base Conversion
Language First — The QR-I Killer, Addressed Directly

An exponential equation is one where the variable appears in the exponent: 4^(x+5) = 8^(x-1). You cannot solve this by standard algebra because x is not multiplied — it is in the power position.

The technique: convert both sides to the same base, then set the exponents equal.

Why does this work? If bᵃ = bᶜ, then a = c — equal powers of the same base must have equal exponents. This is only usable once both sides share the same base.

The hidden step: Before any algebra, ask —
"Can both sides be written as powers of the same number?"

4 = 2² and 8 = 2³ → both are powers of 2
This is the recognition step. Miss it and the equation is unsolvable by this method.
This Is Exactly What Stumped QR-I

The question 4^(x+5) = 8^(x-1) cannot be solved by treating it like a linear equation. The trap is starting to manipulate x without first recognising that 4 and 8 are both powers of 2. The recognition step comes before any algebra. Build the habit: see an exponential equation — ask "what is the common base?" before writing anything else.

Mechanics & Worked Examples
Easy — Solve a Simple Exponential Equation

Solve: 2^x = 32.

Express 32 as a power of 2: 32 = 2^5. So 2^x = 2^5 → x = 5. Verify: 2^5 = 32 ✓

This is the base case for exponential equation solving: recognise both sides as powers of the same base, then set exponents equal. No logarithms required when a common base exists.

Medium — Solving 4^(x+5) = 8^(x-1) — The QR-I Question

Step 1 — Recognise the common base: 4 = 2², 8 = 2³. Both are powers of 2.

Step 2 — Rewrite both sides:

(2²)^(x+5) = (2³)^(x-1)

Step 3 — Apply power rule (multiply exponents):

2^(2(x+5)) = 2^(3(x-1))

Step 4 — Same base, set exponents equal:

2(x+5) = 3(x-1)
2x + 10 = 3x - 3
10 + 3 = 3x - 2x
x = 13

Verify: 4^18 = (2²)^18 = 2^36. 8^12 = (2³)^12 = 2^36. ✓

LEC 18
Rate of Change & Introduction to Derivatives
Language First

The rate of change answers: "How fast is this changing right now?" For a linear function, the rate of change is constant — it is the slope m, the same at every point. For a non-linear or exponential function, the rate of change varies — it is different at each point.

The derivative is the mathematical tool that gives you the instantaneous rate of change at any specific point. This is the topic the QR-II past paper tested in Q5: "find the rate of change of P(t) = 1000e^(0.05t)".

For exponential P(t) = Aeʳᵗ:
Rate of change = dP/dt = rAeʳᵗ = r · P(t)

In plain English: the rate of change of an exponential function
is proportional to the current value.
The bigger the population, the faster it grows.

Basic derivative rules needed:
d/dt (Aeʳᵗ) = rAeʳᵗ
d/dt (aᵗ) = aᵗ · ln(a)
d/dt (tⁿ) = ntⁿ⁻¹ (power rule)
The QR-II Past Paper Q5 — Directly

P(t) = 1000e^(0.05t). "Find the rate of change at any time t" means find dP/dt. Apply the rule: d/dt(Aeʳᵗ) = rAeʳᵗ. Here r=0.05, A=1000. So dP/dt = 0.05 × 1000 × e^(0.05t) = 50e^(0.05t). "Rate of change after 10 years" means evaluate at t=10: dP/dt = 50e^(0.5) ≈ 50 × 1.6487 ≈ 82.4 individuals per year.

Mechanics & Notation
Choosing the right rule — identify the function's shape first:
Variable in the exponent (Aeʳᵗ, aᵗ) → use the exponential derivative rules
Variable in the base, raised to a fixed power (tⁿ) → use the power rule: d/dt(tⁿ) = ntⁿ⁻¹
Sum of terms → differentiate each term separately, then add the results

Procedure for evaluating a derivative at a specific point: first find the general derivative formula (a function of t), then substitute the specific t-value in afterward — never substitute first and differentiate a constant, which gives zero and loses all the information the question is asking for.

Worked Examples
Easy — Power Rule, Single Term

Find the derivative of f(x) = x^4.

Power rule: d/dx[x^n] = n × x^(n-1). Here n=4: f'(x) = 4x^3. At x=2: f'(2) = 4(8) = 32. The rate of change of x^4 at x=2 is 32 — meaning for a tiny increase in x near x=2, the function increases approximately 32 times as fast as x increases.

Medium — Applying the Power Rule — Not Every Rate of Change Is Exponential

A firm's revenue (in thousands of rupees) is modelled as R(t) = 5t² + 30t, where t is months since launch. Find the rate of change of revenue, and find it specifically at t=4 months.

R(t) = 5t² + 30t — sum of two power terms, not exponential
dR/dt = 5(2t) + 30(1) = 10t + 30 — power rule applied to each term separately

At t=4: dR/dt = 10(4) + 30 = 70

Revenue is growing at Rs. 70,000 per month at the 4-month mark. Notice this rate itself changes with t (10t + 30 is not constant) — even though this isn't an exponential model, the rate of change still varies over time, because the underlying function is non-linear. Constant rate of change is a property of linear functions specifically, not the absence of exponential growth.

Hard — Revenue Optimisation, Find Maximum

A firm charges price p and sells Q(p) = 1000 - 10p units. Revenue R(p) = p × Q(p) = p(1000 - 10p) = 1000p - 10p^2. Find the price that maximises revenue.

R'(p) = 1000 - 20p. Set R'(p) = 0: 1000 - 20p = 0 → p = Rs.50. Second derivative: R''(p) = -20 < 0 → maximum confirmed. R(50) = 1000(50) - 10(50)^2 = 50,000 - 25,000 = Rs.25,000 maximum daily revenue.

Legal application: in a price-fixing case, the regulator could argue this firm should charge Rs.50 to maximise revenue. If the firm charges Rs.70, it earns only 1000(70)-10(4900)=70,000-49,000=Rs.21,000 — below its own maximum. This suggests Rs.70 pricing might serve a strategic (market exclusion) rather than profit-maximising purpose, relevant to predatory pricing analysis.

(a) C'(t) = 3t^2 - 24t + 36.

(b) Set 3t^2 - 24t + 36 = 5 → 3t^2 - 24t + 31 = 0. t = (24 ± √(576-372))/6 = (24 ± √204)/6 = (24 ± 14.28)/6. t = 6.38 or t = 1.62. The rate of change equals 5 ppm/year at t≈1.62 and t≈6.38. For t < 1.62 and t > 6.38, C'(t) > 5 (intervention trigger). For 1.62 < t < 6.38, C'(t) < 5.

(c) Decreasing: C'(t) < 0. 3t^2 - 24t + 36 < 0 → t^2 - 8t + 12 < 0 → (t-2)(t-6) < 0 → 2 < t < 6. Pollution actually DECREASES between years 2 and 6.

(d) Company claim evaluation: the company is technically correct that pollution declines during years 2-6 — C'(t) < 0 in that interval. However, this tells only part of the story. After year 6, the decline reverses and pollution grows again (C'(t) > 0 for t > 6). By year 6.38, the rate of change again exceeds 5 ppm/year, triggering intervention. The company is using a temporary declining phase to argue against intervention that will become necessary again. The regulator should require a model projection beyond year 6 to show the upturn — the derivative analysis reveals the "declining pollution" claim is true but incomplete and misleading about the medium-term trajectory.

T3 · CAn expert witness calculates marginal costs for a monopolist: C(q) = q^3 - 6q^2 + 15q + 20, where q is output. The regulator claims: "The firm's marginal cost at q=4 is Rs.3, yet it charges Rs.25. This Rs.22 markup is proof of monopoly power." Defence: "Marginal cost is not the right measure — average cost matters for pricing decisions." (a) Compute MC(4) and average cost AC(4). (b) Evaluate the regulator's claim. (c) Evaluate the defence's claim. (d) Which measure is economically correct for identifying monopoly markup?

(a) MC(q) = C'(q) = 3q^2 - 12q + 15. MC(4) = 3(16) - 12(4) + 15 = 48 - 48 + 15 = 15 (not 3). Regulator computation error. C(4) = 64 - 96 + 60 + 20 = 48. AC(4) = C(4)/q = 48/4 = Rs.12. Price = Rs.25.

(b) Regulator claim: the Rs.3 is wrong — MC(4) = Rs.15, not Rs.3. The regulator made a calculation error. Using the correct MC: markup = 25 - 15 = Rs.10, not Rs.22. Also, a Rs.10 markup over MC in a market with decreasing average costs could reflect normal profit recovery, not necessarily monopoly power. The regulator's claim is built on a computation error and should be corrected before using it in proceedings.

(c) Defence claim: average cost is not the right measure for monopoly pricing analysis. In competitive markets, prices equal MC at equilibrium (P=MC). Monopoly power is typically evidenced by P > MC, not P > AC. Using AC: markup = 25-12 = Rs.13. A firm pricing above AC recovers all costs and earns a profit — that alone does not establish monopoly power. The defence is partly correct that AC alone doesn't prove monopoly, but incorrect that MC is the wrong measure — economic theory uses P-MC (Lerner Index) to measure monopoly power.

(d) Economically correct measure: Lerner Index = (P - MC)/P = (25-15)/25 = 0.40. A Lerner Index of 0.40 (range 0-1) indicates significant market power — 40% of price is markup over marginal cost. The correct analysis uses MC, confirms the regulator's approach is right in principle, but the regulator made a calculation error. The value 15, not 3, is the correct MC(4).

Marginal cost as derivative · average cost calculation · regulator calculation error (MC=15 not 3) · MC vs AC for monopoly analysis · Lerner Index as the correct economic measure · defence claim partly correct but uses wrong measure

The Full Translation Checklist

This is the master checklist for any Part 3 word problem. Read the problem once, then work through these four questions before writing a single equation:

Step 1 — What type of change?
Fixed amount added each period → Linear (y = mt + c)
Fixed percentage each period → Exponential (y = a·bᵗ)
Two unknowns, two constraints → System of linear equations
Optimise subject to limits → Linear programming

Step 2 — Define variables explicitly.
"Let x = ..." before any equation. Always.

Step 3 — Write the model.
Equation(s), objective function, constraints — depending on type.

Step 4 — Answer the specific question asked.
Not "solve the model." Answer the question. Evaluate at the right point,
find the right intersection, interpret the result in context.
Worked Example — Step 1 in Isolation
Easy — Classify, Build, Answer a Simple Legal Question

A company pays a fixed registration fee of Rs.5,000 then Rs.200 per employee. (a) Identify the model type. (b) Write the model. (c) If the total payment is Rs.11,000, how many employees?

(a) Linear — constant Rs.200 per employee means constant addition, not multiplication. (b) C(n) = 200n + 5000. (c) 200n + 5000 = 11000 → 200n = 6000 → n = 30 employees. Step sequence: classify → model → solve. The classification tells you the model type before you write any equations.

Medium — Recognising the Model Before Computing Anything

Step 1 of the checklist — identifying the model type — is where most marks are actually lost, not in the algebra afterward. Four short scenarios, classified on sight, no computation:

(a) "A licensing fee rises by Rs. 2,000 every year." → Linear. Fixed amount, not a percentage.

(b) "Two firms' combined market share is 100%, and Firm A's share is three times Firm B's." → System of linear equations. Two unknowns (the two shares), two stated constraints.

(c) "A fine doubles for each repeated violation." → Exponential. "Doubles" is a multiplicative (percentage-equivalent) change, not a fixed addition.

(d) "A firm wants to maximise profit, subject to a labour cap and a materials budget." → Linear programming. An objective to optimise, plus multiple inequality constraints.

None of these required writing a single equation to classify correctly — the classification comes entirely from recognising the verbal signal (fixed amount, two constraints on two unknowns, multiplicative change, optimisation subject to limits) before any symbol is written down. Get this step right and the remaining three steps are mechanical; get it wrong and no amount of correct algebra recovers the right answer.

Hard — Three-Stage Pipeline with LP and Derivative

A firm must allocate attorney hours (h) between two case types A and B. Revenue: 5000A + 7000B. Constraints: 10A + 15B ≤ 150 hours; A ≥ 3; B ≥ 2. After finding the optimal (A*,B*), the firm asks: "If we add one more hour, which constraint limits us and by how much does revenue increase?"

Stage 1 (LP): Corner points after applying floors A=3, B=2: (3,2): 10(3)+15(2)=60≤150 ✓. Revenue=15000+14000=29,000. (3, (150-30)/15)=(3,8): Revenue=15000+56000=71,000. ((150-30)/10, 2)=(12,2): Revenue=60000+14000=74,000. (3,8) and (12,2) both within floors; check intersection of 10A+15B=150 with A=3: B=8. So corners are (3,8) and (12,2). Optimal: (12,2) at Rs.74,000.

Stage 2 (Rate of change / marginal): If one more hour available (151 hours): 10A+15B ≤ 151 with A=3: B=(151-30)/15=8.07. Revenue=15000+56490=71,490. Or with B=2: A=(151-30)/10=12.1. Revenue=60,500+14000=74,500. Marginal value of an extra hour at (12,2): Rs.500. At (3,8): Rs.490. The hours constraint binds at (12,2); one extra hour adds Rs.500 in revenue.

Stage 3 (Interpret): The optimal is (12,2) — 12 Type-A cases, 2 Type-B cases — despite B having higher per-case revenue (Rs.7,000 vs Rs.5,000), because B requires more hours (15 vs 10). Revenue per hour: A=Rs.500/hr, B=Rs.467/hr. Per-hour efficiency drives the optimal away from the higher-absolute-value case type.

T2 · A A car travels 200 km partly at 40 km/h and partly at 60 km/h. The total time taken is 4 hours. Find the distance travelled at each speed. (This is the QR-I past paper Q2-ii.)

Model type: System of linear equations. Two unknowns, two constraints.

Let d₁ = distance at 40 km/h · d₂ = distance at 60 km/h

Equation 1 (total distance): d₁ + d₂ = 200
Equation 2 (total time): d₁/40 + d₂/60 = 4

From Eq.1: d₁ = 200 - d₂
Sub into Eq.2: (200-d₂)/40 + d₂/60 = 4

Multiply through by 120:
3(200-d₂) + 2d₂ = 480
600 - 3d₂ + 2d₂ = 480
-d₂ = -120 → d₂ = 120 km
d₁ = 200 - 120 = 80 km

Check: 80/40 + 120/60 = 2 + 2 = 4 hours ✓

T3 · A A regulatory agency monitors two factories. Factory A's emissions follow E_A(t) = 80 - 4t (linear reduction from a cleaning programme). Factory B's emissions follow E_B(t) = 60·(0.85)ᵗ (exponential reduction). A limit of 20 units is the legal threshold. Find when each factory falls below the threshold. At t=8, which factory has lower emissions? A lawyer argues: "Factory B is clearly performing better — it started lower and is reducing faster." Evaluate this argument using the mathematics.
Factory A below 20: 80 - 4t = 20 → 4t = 60 → t = 15 years

Factory B below 20: 60(0.85)ᵗ = 20
(0.85)ᵗ = 1/3
t·ln(0.85) = ln(1/3)
t = ln(0.333)/ln(0.85) = -1.0986/-0.1625 ≈ 6.76 years

At t=8:
E_A(8) = 80-32 = 48 units (still above threshold)
E_B(8) = 60(0.85)⁸ = 60(0.2725) ≈ 16.4 units (below threshold ✓)

Evaluating the lawyer's argument: The lawyer's claim has two parts — "started lower" and "reducing faster." Let's test each.

"Started lower": At t=0, E_A=80, E_B=60. True — Factory B started lower.

"Reducing faster": Factory A reduces by 4 units/year (constant linear rate). Factory B's rate of reduction: dE_B/dt = 60·ln(0.85)·(0.85)ᵗ. At t=0: 60·(-0.1625)·1 ≈ -9.75 units/year. Factory B initially reduces faster. But by t=10: dE_B/dt = 60·(-0.1625)·(0.85)^10 ≈ -60·0.1625·0.1969 ≈ -1.92 units/year — slower than Factory A's constant 4 units/year. So "reducing faster" is only true early on.

Overall verdict: The lawyer is correct that Factory B falls below the legal threshold first (~6.76 years vs. 15 years for A). In regulatory terms, Factory B achieves compliance sooner. However, the argument that it is "clearly performing better" based on initial conditions and early rate is an oversimplification — the comparison depends entirely on the time frame and the specific metric (compliance date, total emissions over a period, current level).

(a) Classify each pricing model. (b) Write models. (c) At what search volume do the costs equalise? (d) Which model should the firm choose?

(b) C_sub = 8000 (constant). C_search(n) = 40n where n is searches/month.

(c) 8000 = 40n → n = 200 searches/month. Crossover at exactly 200 searches.

T2 · CAn expert witness presents data: "Property values in this area have grown from Rs.2,000,000 to Rs.3,500,000 over 5 years." The defendant claims: "That's a Rs.300,000/year increase — a 15% annual simple growth rate." The plaintiff claims: "It's compound growth — the annual rate is higher." (a) Verify the defendant's calculation. (b) Compute the compound annual growth rate. (c) Identify which claim is more mathematically defensible and why.

(a) Defendant: (3,500,000 - 2,000,000)/5 = 300,000/year. As a percentage of original: 300,000/2,000,000 = 15%/year simple. Correct arithmetic for linear/simple growth.

(b) Compound annual rate: 2,000,000 × r^5 = 3,500,000 → r^5 = 1.75 → r = 1.75^(0.2) = 1.118 (11.8% compound annual growth rate).

(c) Compound growth (plaintiff) is more mathematically defensible for property values, which compound (each year's growth is on the previous year's inflated value, not the original). However, 11.8% compound is actually LOWER than 15% simple — the plaintiff's claim that compound rate is "higher" is WRONG. Compound rate = 11.8%, simple rate = 15%. The defendant overstates growth using a simple rate; the plaintiff correctly identifies compounding as the right mechanism but incorrectly claims the rate would be higher. The correct answer: compound growth is the right model for property, and the compound rate (11.8%) is the more accurate description of annual growth than the simple rate (15%).

T3 · BFull integrated pipeline: A corporate compliance officer must evaluate whether a company's penalty obligations under a regulatory framework are within manageable range. Base fine: Rs.50,000 for the first violation. Each additional violation increases the fine by 30% (compounding). The firm has had 3 violations so far and expects 2 more in the next year. (a) Classify and model the fine structure. (b) Compute current total fines (for 3 violations). (c) Project fines after 5 violations. (d) Use calculus: at what violation count is the marginal fine Rs.100,000? (e) Advise the compliance officer on the financial risk horizon.

(a) Model type: exponential — each fine is 30% higher than the previous, i.e. multiplicative growth. F(n) = 50,000 × (1.3)^(n-1) where n = violation number (1st violation, 2nd violation, etc.).

(b) Current total for 3 violations: F(1)+F(2)+F(3) = 50,000 + 50,000(1.3) + 50,000(1.3)^2 = 50,000 + 65,000 + 84,500 = Rs.199,500.

(c) After 5 violations: F(4) = 50,000(1.3)^3 = 50,000 × 2.197 = Rs.109,850. F(5) = 50,000(1.3)^4 = 50,000 × 2.856 = Rs.142,800. Total: Rs.199,500 + Rs.109,850 + Rs.142,800 = Rs.452,150.

The exponential structure means each additional violation is materially more expensive than the last. The compliance officer should: (i) treat the 4th violation as a critical threshold (6-figure marginal cost); (ii) allocate disproportionate compliance resources to preventing violations 4 and 5; (iii) note that violation 6 would cost 50,000(1.3)^5=Rs.185,641 — at this rate, violations become existential. Early remediation is exponentially cheaper than incremental tolerance.

T3 · CA large procurement dispute requires full quantitative analysis. A government contractor claims it delivered services worth Rs.1,400,000 over 7 months, billing Rs.200,000/month flat. The government auditor argues: "Your costs declined from Rs.180,000 in month 1 to Rs.60,000 in month 7 as the setup costs amortised — your profit margin grew from 11% to 70%. This is exploitative." The contractor responds: "Our contract specifies a flat monthly fee. Our internal cost structure is irrelevant." (a) Classify the billing model vs the cost model. (b) Build both models from the auditor's data (assuming linear cost decline). (c) Compute profit in each month and cumulative profit over 7 months. (d) Evaluate the legal and mathematical merit of both positions. (e) What additional information determines whether the government has a legal claim?

(a) Billing model: deterministic constant — flat Rs.200,000/month, total = 7 × 200,000 = Rs.1,400,000. Cost model: linear decline — costs decrease from Rs.180,000 (month 1) to Rs.60,000 (month 7), an interval of Rs.120,000 over 6 periods.

(b) Cost model: C(m) = 180,000 - 20,000(m-1) = 200,000 - 20,000m. Check: C(1)=180,000 ✓, C(7)=200,000-140,000=60,000 ✓. Revenue: R(m) = 200,000 (constant).

(c) Monthly profit: π(m) = 200,000 - C(m) = 200,000 - (200,000-20,000m) = 20,000m. Month 1: π=20,000 (11% margin). Month 7: π=140,000 (70% margin). Cumulative: Σπ(m) for m=1 to 7 = 20,000(1+2+3+4+5+6+7) = 20,000 × 28 = Rs.560,000 total profit over 7 months. Average margin: 560,000/1,400,000 = 40%.

(d) Mathematical merit: the auditor's margin calculations are correct. The contractor's response: legally correct in most jurisdictions — unless the contract contains a profit cap clause, a cost-plus adjustment mechanism, or a windfall clawback provision, the margin growth is not exploitative in a fixed-price framework.

(e) Additional information needed: (i) Does the contract contain a profit cap or maximum margin clause? (ii) Is there a cost-plus or cost-reimbursable element to the contract? (iii) Was the government provided with cost estimates at contract signing that significantly differed from actual costs? (iv) Does the jurisdiction's procurement law cap margins on government contracts? Without these, the contractor's position is mathematically correct and legally defensible under standard fixed-price contract law.
