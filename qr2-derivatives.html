RULE 1
The Power Rule
Language First

When you have a variable raised to a fixed number — like x³ or t² or x⁰·⁵ — the power rule tells you the derivative in one step: bring the exponent down as a multiplier, then reduce the exponent by one. That's it. Every polynomial you will ever differentiate uses this rule, and it works for any real-number exponent — positive, negative, fraction, decimal.

The power rule is the most frequently used rule in calculus. Mastering it completely means every polynomial derivative becomes instant.

Where It Applies — and Where It Doesn't

Power rule applies when the variable is in the base and the exponent is a fixed constant: x³, t⁻², x^(½). It does NOT apply when the variable is in the exponent: 3ˣ, eˣ, e^(0.05t) — those need the exponential rule (Rule 5). This base-vs-exponent check is the first translation step every derivatives problem demands.

Mechanics & Notation
Power Rule: d/dx(xⁿ) = n·xⁿ⁻¹

Special cases worth memorising:
d/dx(x¹) = 1            (n=1: bring down 1, reduce to x⁰=1)
d/dx(c) = 0            (constant: n=0, result is 0·x⁻¹ = 0)
d/dx(x½) = ½x⁻½ = 1/(2√x) (square root as fractional power)
d/dx(1/x) = d/dx(x⁻¹) = -x⁻² = -1/x²

Procedure:
Step 1 — Rewrite: convert roots and fractions to xⁿ form
Step 2 — Multiply: bring the exponent down in front
Step 3 — Reduce: subtract 1 from the exponent
Step 4 — Simplify: clean up negative or fractional exponents if needed
Worked Example
Easy — Single Term, Apply Power Rule Directly

Find f’(x) for: (a) f(x) = x⁵ (b) f(x) = 3x² (c) f(x) = 7 (constant)

(a) f’(x) = 5x⁴. (b) f’(x) = 3×2x = 6x. (c) f’(x) = 0. A constant has zero rate of change — its graph is a flat horizontal line, so no slope exists. Power rule: d/dx[cxⁿ] = cnxⁿ⁻¹.

Medium — Three Cases — Positive, Fractional, Negative Exponents

(a) f(x) = x⁵

f'(x) = 5x⁴   (bring down 5, reduce 5→4)

(b) f(x) = √x = x^(½)

f'(x) = ½x^(½−1) = ½x^(−½) = 1/(2√x)

(c) f(x) = 1/x³ = x^(−3)

f'(x) = −3x^(−3−1) = −3x^(−4) = −3/x⁴

In all three, the procedure is identical — rewrite, bring down, reduce by 1. The only difference is the starting form of the exponent. Rewriting as xⁿ before applying the rule avoids the most common errors.

Hard — Legal Cost Optimisation

A law firm’s monthly operating cost: C(x) = 2x³ − 30x² + 120x + 500 (Rs. thousands, x = staff count). Find the staffing level that minimises marginal cost.

Marginal cost: MC(x) = C’(x) = 6x² − 60x + 120. Rate of change of MC: MC’(x) = 12x − 60. Set MC’(x) = 0: 12x = 60, x = 5. At x=5 marginal cost is at its minimum inflection. MC(5) = 6(25)−300+120 = 150−300+120 = Rs.−30 (negative marginal cost, meaning MC is still falling at x=5, which is the minimum of the MC curve). This tells the managing partner: adding staff is cheapest at the 5th hire; beyond that, each additional hire costs more per unit. Optimal staff count from a pure MC perspective is around 5, subject to revenue constraints.

NY Bar · Expert Witness Connection

Marginal analysis — which is simply the derivative evaluated at a specific point — is the core language of economics expert testimony in antitrust and damages litigation. A lawyer who can independently compute the marginal cost or marginal revenue figure the expert reports, from the underlying cost function, can verify it, challenge it, and cross-examine it on the stand rather than relying on the expert's number uncritically.

The "last mile" problem in environmental cleanup (T3 above) recurs across multiple bodies of law: remediation consent decrees, ADA architectural compliance timelines, and food safety corrective action plans all involve scenarios where the final percentage of compliance is exponentially more expensive than the first 90%, and the derivative's rate-of-change behaviour is the mathematical basis for every cost estimate the regulator and the regulated party will dispute.

RULE 2
The Addition & Subtraction Rule
Language First

When a function is built by adding or subtracting several pieces, its derivative is simply the sum (or difference) of each piece's individual derivative. Each term is handled separately, independently of the others. The rule is as simple as it sounds, and the only way to apply it wrong is to panic when a function has many terms and try to treat it as one complicated whole rather than a collection of simple pieces.

This rule also underlies why the power rule alone handles any polynomial — a polynomial is just several power-rule terms added together, so you differentiate each one and add the results.

Mechanics & Notation
Sum/Difference Rule:
d/dx[f(x) ± g(x)] = f'(x) ± g'(x)

Extended to any number of terms:
d/dx[u + v − w + z] = u' + v' − w' + z'

Constants ride through:
d/dx[5x³] = 5 · d/dx[x³] = 5 · 3x² = 15x²
(A constant multiplier stays attached to its term throughout)

The only common slip: forgetting to carry the constant multiplier all the way through when combining with the power rule. The constant never disappears — it just multiplies whatever the power rule produces.

Worked Example
Easy — Differentiate a Polynomial Term by Term

f(x) = x⁴ + 3x² − 7x + 2. Find f’(x).

Apply power rule to each term independently: d/dx[x⁴]=4x³, d/dx[3x²]=6x, d/dx[−7x]=−7, d/dx[2]=0. Sum: f’(x) = 4x³ + 6x − 7. The constant 2 disappears; the −7x term gives a constant slope contribution of −7.

Medium — Full Polynomial Differentiation

A law firm's total cost function is C(x) = 4x³ − 9x² + 12x − 7, where x is the number of cases handled per month. Find the marginal cost function C'(x) and the marginal cost at x = 3.

C'(x) = d/dx(4x³) − d/dx(9x²) + d/dx(12x) − d/dx(7)
      = 4(3x²) − 9(2x) + 12(1) − 0
      = 12x² − 18x + 12

At x = 3: C'(3) = 12(9) − 18(3) + 12 = 108 − 54 + 12 = 66

The marginal cost of handling the 3rd additional case is Rs. 66 (in whatever units C is expressed). Notice d/dx(7) = 0 — constants disappear on differentiation, because a fixed overhead cost doesn't change as you add one more case.

Hard — Combined Revenue-Cost Model, Find Break-Even Rate of Change

Revenue: R(q) = −2q² + 60q. Cost: C(q) = q² − 10q + 200. Profit: Π(q) = R(q) − C(q). Find the output that maximises profit and the price at which a firm breaking even has zero marginal profit.

Π(q) = (−2q²+60q) − (q²−10q+200) = −3q² + 70q − 200. Π’(q) = −6q + 70. Max profit: −6q+70=0, q=70/6≈11.67 units. Π(11.67) = −3(136)+70(11.67)−200 = −408+816.9−200 = Rs.208.9 thousand. Break-even (Π=0): −3q²+70q−200=0. q=(70±√(4900−2400))/(−6) = (70±50)/(6). q=20 or q≈3.33. Marginal profit at break-even: Π’(3.33)=−6(3.33)+70=50, Π’(20)=−6(20)+70=−50. At the lower break-even (q=3.33), marginal profit is positive (still worth producing more). At the upper break-even (q=20), marginal profit is negative (the firm is past its profitable range).

RULE 3
The Product Rule
Language First

When two functions are multiplied together, the derivative is not simply the product of their individual derivatives — this is the single most common misconception in introductory calculus and it is wrong every time. Instead: "differentiate the first, keep the second — then keep the first, differentiate the second" and add the two results.

The Wrong Version to Watch For

h'(x) ≠ u'(x)·v'(x). If your answer is the product of two derivatives, you've applied the wrong rule. The correct answer is always a sum of two terms.

Mechanics & Notation
Product Rule: if h(x) = u(x)·v(x), then
h'(x) = u'(x)·v(x) + u(x)·v'(x)

Procedure: Label u and v → find u' and v' separately → assemble u'v + uv' → simplify
Worked Example
Easy — Identify u and v, Apply Directly

h(x) = x²(x+3). Find h’(x) using the product rule.

u=x², u’=2x; v=x+3, v’=1. h’ = u’v+uv’ = 2x(x+3)+x²(1) = 2x²+6x+x² = 3x²+6x. Verify by expanding first: h(x)=x³+3x², h’=3x²+6x. ✓

Medium — Product Rule — h(x) = x³·eˣ
u = x³, v = eˣ → u' = 3x², v' = eˣ
h'(x) = 3x²·eˣ + x³·eˣ = eˣ(3x² + x³) = x²eˣ(3 + x)

Notice v' = eˣ because the derivative of eˣ is itself. The factoring step is optional but clean — the un-factored form is fully correct on an exam.

Hard — Liability Model: Product Rule to Find Maximum

Liability: L(x)=x²·e^⁻ˣ. L’(x)=e^⁻ˣ(2x−x²)=xe^⁻ˣ(2−x). L’=0 at x=2 (and x=0). L’’(2)<0 confirms maximum at x=2. L(2)=4e^⁻²≈0.54. For x>2, detection probability falls faster than severity grows — total liability declines. A fine schedule based on this model creates perverse incentives for high-severity offences (x>2): penalties decrease. Regulatory design error.

RULE 4
The Quotient Rule
Language First

When one function is divided by another, use the quotient rule. The subtraction order in the numerator matters — reverse it and every sign is wrong. Memory hook: "low d-high minus high d-low, square the bottom and away we go."

Mechanics & Notation
Quotient Rule: if h(x) = u(x)/v(x), then
h'(x) = [u'(x)·v(x) − u(x)·v'(x)] / [v(x)]²

Procedure: Label u (high) and v (low) → find u', v' → assemble [u'v − uv'] → divide by v²
Worked Example
Easy — Identify Numerator/Denominator, Apply Directly

h(x) = x²/(x+1). u=x², u’=2x; v=x+1, v’=1. h’ = (2x(x+1)−x²)/(x+1)² = (x²+2x)/(x+1)². Always: u’v MINUS uv’ in numerator (not the reverse).

Medium — Quotient Rule — h(x) = (x²+1)/(x−3)
u = x²+1, v = x−3 → u' = 2x, v' = 1
h'(x) = [2x(x−3) − (x²+1)(1)] / (x−3)²
= [2x²−6x−x²−1] / (x−3)² = (x²−6x−1) / (x−3)²

The numerator is purely algebraic simplification after the rule is applied — expand, collect like terms, done.

Hard — Average Cost Minimisation

AC(q) = q²−6q+11+50/q (after dividing C(q)=q³−6q²+11q+50 by q). AC’=2q−6−50/q²=0 → 2q³−6q²−50=0. Trial: q≈4.3 gives AC≈15.3. AC’’=2+100/q³>0 confirms minimum. RULE 5
The Chain Rule
Language First

The chain rule handles functions inside other functions. The signal: a standard function (power, exponential, log) applied not to a bare x but to a whole expression — like (3x²+1)⁵, e^(x²), ln(4x+7). Rule: differentiate the outside first, leaving the inside untouched — then multiply by the derivative of the inside.

Most Commonly Forgotten Step

Students differentiate the outer correctly but forget to multiply by the inner derivative. Always ask: "did I multiply by the derivative of what's inside?"

Mechanics & Notation
Chain Rule: if y = f(g(x)), then dy/dx = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

Common forms:
d/dx[(g(x))ⁿ] = n(g(x))ⁿ⁻¹ · g'(x)
d/dx[e^(g(x))] = e^(g(x)) · g'(x)
d/dx[ln(g(x))] = g'(x)/g(x)

Procedure: identify outer f and inner g → differentiate outer (inner stays intact) → differentiate inner → multiply
Worked Example
Easy — Identify Inner/Outer, Apply Directly

h(x) = (x²+3)⁵. Outer: u⁵, inner: u=x²+3. h’(x) = 5(x²+3)⁴ · 2x = 10x(x²+3)⁴. Always multiply the outer derivative (in terms of u) by du/dx.

(a) y = (3x²+5)⁴

Outer (·)⁴ → 4(·)³ · Inner g'=6x → dy/dx = 24x(3x²+5)³

Outer e^(·) → e^(·) · Inner g'=15x² → dy/dx = 15x²e^(5x³)

(c) y = ln(x²+1)

Outer ln(·) → 1/(·) · Inner g'=2x → dy/dx = 2x/(x²+1)

RULE 6
The Exponential Rule
Language First

The exponential function eˣ has a unique and extraordinary property: its derivative is itself. It is the only function in mathematics that is unchanged by differentiation. This is not a coincidence — it is the defining property of e as a number, and the reason e appears in every natural growth and decay model.

For a general base a (not e), the derivative introduces a factor of ln a — the natural logarithm of the base — because any base can be rewritten in terms of e, and that conversion leaves a ln a factor behind.

Mechanics & Notation
Base e: d/dx(eˣ) = eˣ
Base e, scaled: d/dx(Aeʳˣ) = rAeʳˣ   (chain rule applies — r is the inner derivative)
General base a: d/dx(aˣ) = aˣ · ln a
General base a, scaled: d/dx(aʳˣ) = r · aʳˣ · ln a

Why ln a appears: aˣ = e^(x ln a), so d/dx(e^(x ln a)) = e^(x ln a) · ln a = aˣ · ln a
Vs the Power Rule — Critical Distinction

d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹ — variable in the base, constant in the exponent → Power Rule.
d/dx(aˣ) = aˣ ln a — constant in the base, variable in the exponent → Exponential Rule.
Getting these backwards is the single most tested point of confusion across all derivatives problems.

Worked Example
Easy — Differentiate eˣ and aˣ Directly

(a) f(x)=e^(5x): d/dx[e^(5x)]=5e^(5x). (b) g(x)=3ˣ: d/dx[3ˣ]=3ˣ·ln(3). Rule: d/dx[e^(kx)]=ke^(kx); d/dx[aˣ]=aˣ·ln(a). The ln(a) factor accounts for the change of base from e.

Medium — Base e and General Base — Both Forms

(a) f(x) = 500e^(0.07x) — continuous compound growth at 7%

f'(x) = 0.07 × 500 × e^(0.07x) = 35e^(0.07x)
At x=10: f'(10) = 35e^(0.7) ≈ 35 × 2.0138 ≈ 70.48 units/period

(b) g(x) = 3ˣ — base 3, general exponential

g'(x) = 3ˣ · ln 3 ≈ 3ˣ × 1.0986
At x=2: g'(2) = 9 × ln 3 ≈ 9.887

Compare: if you mistakenly applied the power rule to 3ˣ, you'd get x·3^(x−1), which is completely wrong — the power rule requires the base to be the variable. This mistake gives a structurally different (and incorrect) function shape.

RULE 7
The Natural Logarithm Rule
Language First

The natural logarithm ln(x) is the inverse of eˣ — they undo each other. Their derivatives mirror this inverse relationship: where eˣ differentiates to itself (exponential growth), ln(x) differentiates to 1/x (which declines toward zero) — the rate of a logarithm slows continuously, which is exactly what "logarithmic growth" means in plain English.

ln(x) appears in economics, information theory, entropy calculations, and growth models wherever the rate of change is proportional to the reciprocal of the current level. In legal and statistical contexts, it also appears in the log-likelihood functions that underlie many expert statistical models — knowing how ln differentiates is prerequisite to understanding how those models are fitted.

Mechanics & Notation
Natural log rule: d/dx(ln x) = 1/x   (x > 0)

With a constant multiple: d/dx(a·ln x) = a/x

Combined with chain rule (composite):
d/dx[ln(g(x))] = g'(x)/g(x)

Domain note: ln x is only defined for x > 0. For expressions like ln|x|, the derivative is still 1/x but the domain extends to x ≠ 0. In word problems, the domain is almost always positive by context.
Worked Example
Easy — Apply ln Rule Directly

(a) f(x)=ln(x): f’(x)=1/x. (b) g(x)=ln(3x): g’(x)=1/x (the constant inside disappears). Verify: ln(3x)=ln(3)+ln(x), derivative = 0+1/x. (c) h(x)=ln(x²)=2ln(x): h’(x)=2/x. Remember: d/dx[ln(u)]=u’/u.

Medium — Natural Log — Simple and Composite Forms

(a) f(x) = 3 ln x

f'(x) = 3 · (1/x) = 3/x

(b) f(x) = ln(x² + 3x)

Chain rule: outer ln(·) → 1/(·), inner g = x²+3x → g' = 2x+3
f'(x) = (2x+3)/(x²+3x) = (2x+3)/(x(x+3))

(c) f(x) = x²·ln x — requires product rule AND natural log rule

u = x², v = ln x → u' = 2x, v' = 1/x
f'(x) = 2x·ln x + x²·(1/x) = 2x ln x + x = x(2 ln x + 1)
Hard — Optimal Penalty via Log Model

RULE 8
The Logarithm Rule (Base a)
Language First

The log base-a rule is the generalisation of the natural log rule to any base. It introduces a 1/ln a factor in the denominator — the same ln a that appeared in the general exponential rule's numerator. This is not a coincidence: log base a and exponential base a are inverses of each other, and their derivative rules are mirrors.

In practice, log base 10 (written log₁₀ or simply log) appears most often in measurement scales — the Richter scale for earthquakes, the decibel scale for sound, and pH in chemistry are all base-10 logarithms. Log base 2 appears in information theory and data compression. For any of these, the derivative is the base-a rule.

Mechanics & Notation
Log base-a rule: d/dx(logₐ x) = 1/(x · ln a)

Most common case — base 10: d/dx(log₁₀ x) = 1/(x · ln 10) ≈ 1/(x · 2.3026)

Why the ln a denominator? logₐ x = ln x / ln a (change-of-base formula). Differentiating: d/dx(ln x / ln a) = (1/x) · (1/ln a) = 1/(x ln a). The ln a in the denominator comes from dividing by a constant.

Combined with chain rule: d/dx[logₐ(g(x))] = g'(x) / (g(x) · ln a)
Worked Example
Easy — Apply log_a Rule Directly

(a) f(x)=log_{10}(x): f’(x)=1/(x·ln(10))=1/(2.303x). (b) g(x)=log_2(x): g’(x)=1/(x·ln(2))=1/(0.693x). Rule: d/dx[log_a(x)]=1/(x·ln(a)). ln(a) is a constant that "scales" the natural log derivative. When a=e, ln(e)=1 and we recover d/dx[ln(x)]=1/x.

Medium — Log Base-a and Base-10 Derivatives

f'(x) = 1/(x · ln 10) ≈ 1/(2.3026x)

(b) f(x) = log₂(3x² + 1)

f'(x) = 6x / ((3x²+1) · ln 2) = 6x / (0.6931(3x²+1))

Comparison — why ln is usually preferred: d/dx(ln x) = 1/x, clean. d/dx(log₁₀ x) = 1/(x ln 10), an extra constant in the denominator always. In modelling contexts, engineers and scientists use ln specifically to avoid this constant — it's not that ln is more important, it's that its derivative is simpler. When you see log₁₀ in a real-world problem, the fastest approach is often to convert via log₁₀(x) = ln(x)/ln(10) before differentiating.

(b) If k=50, a=10, find the concentration at which dH/dc = −1 (a "critical rate"). (c) The regulator wants to choose a (the base) so that the critical rate occurs at c=20. Find a. (d) A lawyer argues: "Changing the base only changes the scale of H, not when the derivative equals −1." Is this correct?

(a) H=−k·log_a(c)=−k·[ln(c)/ln(a)]. dH/dc=−k·[1/(c·ln(a))]=−k/(c·ln(a)). ✓

(b) k=50, a=10: −50/(c·ln(10))=−1 → c=50/ln(10)=50/2.303=21.7 ppm. Critical rate at c=21.7 ppm with standard parameters.

(c) Want critical rate at c=20: −50/(20·ln(a))=−1 → ln(a)=50/20=2.5 → a=e^2.5=12.18. The regulator should use base a≈12.18 to shift the critical concentration from 21.7 to 20 ppm.

(d) Lawyer claim: wrong. Changing a DOES change when dH/dc=−1, as demonstrated in (b) vs (c): a=10 gives critical concentration 21.7, a=12.18 gives 20. The base a appears in ln(a) in the denominator of dH/dc, so it directly affects both the scale of H AND the rate at which H changes. The lawyer is confusing the effect of the base on H(c) values (which is a rescaling) with the effect on dH/dc (which is not a simple rescaling — it shifts the critical concentration).
