PROB 01
Foundations of Probability — Sample Space, Rules, and Conditional Probability
Language First

Probability is a number between 0 and 1 that measures how likely an event is to occur. P = 0 means impossible; P = 1 means certain. The sample space S is the set of all possible outcomes. An event A is any subset of S.

Three interpretations: Classical — equal likelihood (flipping a fair coin). Empirical/Frequentist — long-run relative frequency (30% of defendants are convicted based on historical data). Subjective — a personal degree of belief (a lawyer believes there is a 70% chance of winning).

Why Probability Precedes Statistics

Every statistical procedure in this course — confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, chi-square — produces a number that is a probability (the p-value) or uses probability to define a range (the CI). Without a foundation in what probability means, these numbers cannot be correctly interpreted. Probability is the language; statistics is what you say with it.

Mechanics & Notation
Classical probability: P(A) = |A| / |S| = favourable outcomes / total outcomes
Complement rule: P(A') = 1 − P(A)   [A' = "not A"]
Addition rule (general): P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A∩B)
Addition rule (mutually exclusive): P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B)   [if A∩B = ∅]
Multiplication rule (independent): P(A∩B) = P(A) × P(B)
Multiplication rule (general): P(A∩B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
Conditional probability: P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B)   [P(B) > 0]
Independence test: A and B are independent iff P(A|B) = P(A)
Mutually Exclusive vs Independent — Most Confused Pair in Probability

Mutually exclusive: if A occurs, B cannot. P(A∩B) = 0. Independent: whether A occurs tells you nothing about B. P(A|B) = P(A). These are NOT the same thing — in fact, two events with nonzero probability cannot be both mutually exclusive AND independent (if A∩B=∅ then P(A|B)=0≠P(A)). On an exam: "mutually exclusive" means use the simple addition rule; "independent" means use the simple multiplication rule.

Worked Examples
Easy — Classical Probability and Complement Rule

A jury pool of 80 people contains 30 lawyers, 25 doctors, and 25 teachers. One person is selected at random. Find: (a) P(lawyer), (b) P(not a lawyer), (c) P(lawyer or doctor).

(a) P(lawyer) = 30/80 = 3/8 = 0.375

(b) P(not lawyer) = 1 − 0.375 = 0.625. Or directly: 50/80 = 0.625.

(c) Lawyer and doctor are mutually exclusive (cannot be both simultaneously in this sample). P(lawyer or doctor) = 30/80 + 25/80 = 55/80 = 0.6875. No subtraction needed because they do not overlap.

Medium — Addition Rule with Overlap

In a criminal court database of 1000 cases: 350 defendants had a prior record (P), 200 cases involved a weapon (W), and 80 cases had both. Find: (a) P(prior record or weapon), (b) P(neither), (c) P(weapon | prior record).

(a) P(P∪W) = P(P) + P(W) − P(P∩W) = 350/1000 + 200/1000 − 80/1000 = 0.35 + 0.20 − 0.08 = 0.47

(b) P(neither) = 1 − P(P∪W) = 1 − 0.47 = 0.53

(c) P(W|P) = P(W∩P)/P(P) = 0.08/0.35 = 0.229. Among defendants with prior records, 22.9% also used a weapon — higher than the overall weapon rate of 20%, suggesting a mild positive association.

Hard — Independence Test and Multiplication Rule

A regulatory body reviews two independent inspectors' reports on a factory. Inspector A correctly identifies a violation with probability 0.7. Inspector B correctly identifies a violation with probability 0.8. The inspectors work independently. Find: (a) P(both identify the violation), (b) P(at least one identifies it), (c) P(neither identifies it), (d) Are the events "A identifies it" and "B identifies it" mutually exclusive?

(a) Independent → multiply: P(A∩B) = 0.7 × 0.8 = 0.56

(b) P(at least one) = 1 − P(neither) = 1 − (1−0.7)(1−0.8) = 1 − 0.3×0.2 = 1 − 0.06 = 0.94. Or: P(A) + P(B) − P(A∩B) = 0.7 + 0.8 − 0.56 = 0.94 ✓

(c) P(neither) = P(A')×P(B') = 0.3 × 0.2 = 0.06. There is a 6% chance a genuine violation goes undetected by both.

(d) Not mutually exclusive. Both CAN happen simultaneously (probability 0.56). Mutual exclusivity would require P(A∩B)=0. Legal implication: when two independent inspectors both report a violation, it is highly credible — the probability of a false double-positive is very small if each is accurate.

T2 · CTwo courts independently decide an appeal. Court A rules in plaintiff's favour with probability 0.6. Court B does so with probability 0.7. (a) P(both rule for plaintiff). (b) P(at least one rules for plaintiff). (c) P(exactly one rules for plaintiff).

T2 · DExplain the difference between mutually exclusive events and independent events. Give a legal example of each. Can two events with non-zero probability be both mutually exclusive AND independent?

Mutually exclusive: A and B cannot happen together — P(A∩B)=0. Legal example: in a single verdict, "guilty" and "not guilty" are mutually exclusive. If one occurs, the other cannot.

Independent: Whether A occurs gives no information about B — P(A|B)=P(A). Legal example: whether juror 1 voted guilty and whether juror 2 voted guilty are (ideally) independent — each deliberates separately.

Cannot be both (if non-zero): If A∩B=∅ then P(A∩B)=0. For independence we need P(A∩B)=P(A)×P(B). If P(A)>0 and P(B)>0, then P(A)×P(B)>0≠0. Contradiction. So mutually exclusive events with non-zero probabilities are necessarily dependent — knowing A occurred tells you B definitely did not.

Conceptual distinction · legal examples · proof that ME and independent are incompatible for non-zero probabilities
T3 · AA factory's product liability claim rests on two defects: a design defect (D) and a manufacturing defect (M). P(D) = 0.15, P(M) = 0.25. An engineer claims the defects are independent. (a) Under independence, find P(D∩M), P(D∪M). (b) Investigation shows 120 of 500 products have at least one defect, and 10 have both. Is the independence assumption justified? (c) Compute the actual conditional probability P(M|D) from the data and compare to P(M). What does the discrepancy tell the court?

(a) Under independence: P(D∩M) = 0.15 × 0.25 = 0.0375. P(D∪M) = 0.15 + 0.25 − 0.0375 = 0.3625. So we'd expect 36.25% of products to have at least one defect and 3.75% to have both.

(b) From data: P(D∪M) = 120/500 = 0.24. P(D∩M) = 10/500 = 0.02. Expected under independence: 36.25% and 3.75%. Actual: 24% and 2%. Both are substantially lower than the independence prediction — but also, P(D∩M) = 0.02 ≠ P(D)×P(M) = 0.0375. The independence assumption is not justified — the defects are negatively associated (both defects occurring together is less frequent than independence would predict).

(c) P(M|D) = P(M∩D)/P(D) = 0.02/0.15 = 0.133. P(M) = 0.25. Since P(M|D) = 0.133 ≠ P(M) = 0.25, the events are dependent. Knowing there is a design defect actually lowers the probability of a manufacturing defect from 25% to 13.3%. Legal implication: the defects are negatively correlated — perhaps design-defective products go through enhanced manufacturing inspection. The company cannot claim the defects are independent, but equally, the plaintiff cannot argue they compound each other — the data shows they don't co-occur as often as independent chance would predict.

Independence assumption testing · comparing predicted vs observed joint probability · negative association from P(M|D) < P(M) · legal implication of dependence direction
T3 · BA criminal investigation: 40% of suspects have a motive (M), 30% have opportunity (O), and 15% have both. (a) Are motive and opportunity independent? (b) P(motive | opportunity). (c) P(no motive and no opportunity). (d) A detective argues: "Since most people with opportunity also have motive, this suspect's opportunity is strong evidence of guilt." Evaluate this reasoning using the conditional probabilities.

(a) P(M)×P(O) = 0.40×0.30 = 0.12 ≠ P(M∩O) = 0.15. Not independent — motive and opportunity are positively associated.

(b) P(M|O) = 0.15/0.30 = 0.50. Half of those with opportunity also have motive — higher than the base rate of 40% for motive.

(c) P(M'∩O') = 1 − P(M∪O) = 1 − (0.40+0.30−0.15) = 1 − 0.55 = 0.45. 45% of suspects have neither.

(d) The detective's reasoning is partially flawed. P(M|O) = 0.50 means half of those with opportunity have motive — that is elevated above the base rate (0.40) showing some association. But the detective says "most" have motive when opportunity is present — 50% is not "most." More importantly, having both motive and opportunity does not establish guilt — it merely narrows the suspect pool. P(M∩O) = 0.15 means 15% of suspects have both — a significant group, not a unique identifier. The conditional probability shows elevated risk, not certainty. This is a base rate neglect argument: even among those with both M and O, the vast majority are innocent (assuming a small true guilty rate in the population).

Independence test · P(M|O)=0.50 vs P(M)=0.40 · positive association · "most" misrepresentation · base rate neglect in legal reasoning
T3 · CA quality audit finds that 5% of products are defective. A testing machine correctly identifies a defective product as defective (sensitivity) 90% of the time and correctly identifies a good product as good (specificity) 95% of the time. (a) A product tests positive (machine says defective). Using a probability tree, find P(actually defective | tests positive). (b) A product tests negative. Find P(actually defective | tests negative). (c) The manufacturer argues the machine is 90% accurate — evaluate this claim using your results.

Define: D = defective, D' = good, + = positive test, − = negative test. P(D)=0.05, P(+|D)=0.90, P(−|D')=0.95 → P(+|D')=0.05.

(a) P(D∩+) = P(D)×P(+|D) = 0.05×0.90 = 0.045. P(D'∩+) = P(D')×P(+|D') = 0.95×0.05 = 0.0475. P(+) = 0.045+0.0475 = 0.0925. P(D|+) = 0.045/0.0925 = 0.486. A positive test means there is only a 48.6% chance the product is actually defective — barely above 50%!

(b) P(D∩−) = 0.05×0.10 = 0.005. P(D'∩−) = 0.95×0.95 = 0.9025. P(−) = 0.9075. P(D|−) = 0.005/0.9075 = 0.0055. A negative test leaves only a 0.55% chance of actual defect — the machine is excellent at ruling out defects.

(c) The "90% accurate" claim refers to sensitivity (90% of defectives are caught). But for a product that tests positive, there is only a 48.6% chance it is truly defective — slightly worse than a coin flip. This is because defectives are rare (5%) — the machine produces almost as many false positives (4.75%) as true positives (4.5%). The manufacturer's 90% claim is misleading in context: the relevant metric for a positive test result is the positive predictive value (48.6%), not sensitivity. This is the base rate problem applied to quality control — exactly the same logic as medical testing or DNA evidence.

Probability tree · P(D|+)=48.6% despite 90% sensitivity · P(D|−)=0.55% excellent at ruling out · PPV vs sensitivity distinction · base rate drives reliability

PROB 02
Normal Distribution — The Bell Curve, Z-Scores, and Table Reading
Language First

The Normal distribution is the most important distribution in statistics. It is bell-shaped, symmetric around its mean μ, and fully described by two parameters: mean μ (centre) and standard deviation σ (spread). It is written X ~ N(μ, σ²).

Many real phenomena are approximately normally distributed: heights, test scores, measurement errors, and — after large samples — sample means (Central Limit Theorem). In law, sentence lengths, fine amounts, income levels, and response times are often modelled as normal.

The Z-Score — One Formula That Solves Everything

Any normal distribution problem reduces to one step: convert to the standard normal Z ~ N(0,1) using z = (x − μ)/σ. Then look up P(Z < z) in the table. All probability questions about any normal distribution go through this single conversion.

Mechanics & Notation
Distribution: X ~ N(μ, σ²)   μ = mean, σ = standard deviation
Z-score (standardise): z = (x − μ) / σ
Standard normal: Z ~ N(0, 1)
P(X < x): = P(Z < z) = Φ(z) — read directly from table
P(X > x): = 1 − Φ(z)
P(a < X < b): = Φ(z_b) − Φ(z_a) where z_a=(a−μ)/σ, z_b=(b−μ)/σ
Symmetry: Φ(−z) = 1 − Φ(z)   [use for negative z values]
Inverse (find x given prob p): x = μ + z_p × σ

The 68–95–99.7 Rule (Empirical Rule)

P(μ−σ < X < μ+σ) = 0.6827 (68.27%) · P(μ−2σ < X < μ+2σ) = 0.9545 (95.45%) · P(μ−3σ < X < μ+3σ) = 0.9973 (99.73%). Memorise these — they give you instant approximate answers without a table and are directly tested in short questions.

Worked Examples
Easy — Compute Z-Score and Find P(X < x)

Sentence lengths in a jurisdiction are normally distributed with μ = 5 years, σ = 1.5 years. Find the probability that a randomly selected sentence is less than 7.5 years.

Step 1 — Z-score: z = (7.5 − 5)/1.5 = 2.5/1.5 = 1.67

Step 2 — Table: P(Z < 1.67) ≈ 0.9525 (between 1.65 → 0.9505 and 2.00 → 0.9772; closer to 0.9505).

Answer: P(X < 7.5) ≈ 0.9525. About 95.25% of sentences are shorter than 7.5 years. A 7.5-year sentence is at the 95th percentile — unusually long relative to the norm.

Medium — P(a < X < b) Between Two Values

Using the same distribution (μ=5, σ=1.5): what proportion of sentences fall between 3.5 and 6.5 years?

Z for 3.5: z₁ = (3.5 − 5)/1.5 = −1.5/1.5 = −1.00 → Φ(−1.00) = 1 − 0.8413 = 0.1587

Z for 6.5: z₂ = (6.5 − 5)/1.5 = 1.5/1.5 = 1.00 → Φ(1.00) = 0.8413

P(3.5 < X < 6.5) = Φ(1.00) − Φ(−1.00) = 0.8413 − 0.1587 = 0.6826 ≈ 68.3%

This is exactly the Empirical Rule (μ ± 1σ contains 68.27%). The range 3.5 to 6.5 is precisely μ ± σ. A defence attorney could argue that any sentence outside this range (below 3.5 or above 6.5 years) is statistically atypical for this jurisdiction.

Hard — Inverse Problem: Find x Given Probability

A financial regulator classifies fines as "excessive" if they fall in the top 5% of the distribution for the sector. Fines are N(μ=50,000, σ=12,000). Find the excessive threshold.

We need x such that P(X > x) = 0.05, i.e. P(X < x) = 0.95.

Find z: From table, Φ(1.65) = 0.9505 ≈ 0.95. So z ≈ 1.65.

Back-transform: x = μ + z × σ = 50,000 + 1.65 × 12,000 = 50,000 + 19,800 = Rs. 69,800.

Any fine above Rs. 69,800 is in the top 5% and would be classified as excessive. The regulator can use this as a bright-line threshold — not arbitrary, but based on the empirical distribution of sector fines. Using z=1.96 (for top 2.5%): threshold = 50,000 + 1.96×12,000 = Rs. 73,520.

T2 · BAverage case resolution time is N(μ=45, σ=8) days. A court order requires resolution within 60 days. (a) What proportion of cases comply? (b) The chief justice wants 99% compliance. What maximum resolution time should be set?

(a) z=(60−45)/8=15/8=1.875. Φ(1.875)≈Φ(1.88)≈0.9699. About 97% of cases comply with the 60-day limit.

(b) Need Φ(z)=0.99 → z≈2.33 (from table: Φ(2.33)=0.9901≈0.99). Time limit = μ+z×σ = 45+2.33×8 = 45+18.64 = 63.6 days ≈ 64 days. Setting the limit at 64 days achieves 99% compliance under this distribution.

T2 · CWhat is the 68–95–99.7 rule? A judge is told: "The fine imposed is 2.5 standard deviations above the mean for this category of offence." Without a table, what approximate probability can the judge assign to a fine this extreme or larger? What is the precise value from the table?

68–95–99.7 Rule: Under a normal distribution, approximately 68% of values fall within 1σ of the mean, 95% within 2σ, and 99.7% within 3σ. These are exact probabilities: P(μ−σ<X<μ+σ)=0.6827, P(μ−2σ<X<μ+2σ)=0.9545, P(μ−3σ<X<μ+3σ)=0.9973.

Approximation for z=2.5: Between 2σ (95%) and 3σ (99.7%) bounds, so P(X>μ+2.5σ) is between (1−0.9545)/2=0.023 and (1−0.9973)/2=0.0014. Rough estimate: about 0.6%.

Precise value: P(Z>2.5)=1−Φ(2.5)=1−0.9938=0.0062. About 0.62% of fines exceed this level — i.e. roughly 1 in 160 cases. This is statistically rare and would support an "excessive" characterisation.

68-95-99.7 rule · approximation without table · precise P(Z>2.5)=0.0062 · statistical rarity in legal context
T2 · DIncome in a district is N(μ=35,000, σ=8,000) Rs. per month. The poverty line is Rs. 20,000. (a) What proportion of residents are below the poverty line? (b) The government wants to target the bottom 10% for subsidies. What income threshold defines eligibility?

(a) z=(20,000−35,000)/8,000=−15,000/8,000=−1.875. P(Z<−1.875)=1−Φ(1.875)≈1−0.9699=0.0301. About 3% of residents fall below the poverty line.

(b) Need P(X<x)=0.10 → Φ(z)=0.10 → z=−1.28 (since Φ(1.28)=0.8997, Φ(−1.28)=0.1003≈0.10). Income threshold = 35,000+(−1.28)×8,000 = 35,000−10,240 = Rs. 24,760. Residents earning below Rs. 24,760 qualify for subsidies, representing the bottom 10% of this income distribution.

T3 · AA drug concentration in blood follows N(μ=8, σ=1.5) mg/L. (a) Legal impairment threshold is 10 mg/L. What % of tested subjects exceed it? (b) A roadside test registers "positive" for any reading above 7 mg/L (set low to catch most cases). What % of tests are positive? (c) Of those testing positive, what proportion actually exceed the legal threshold? (d) A defence lawyer argues the roadside test produces "massive false positives." Evaluate using your calculations.

(a) z=(10−8)/1.5=1.333. P(Z>1.333)≈1−Φ(1.33)≈1−0.9082=0.0918. About 9.2% of subjects exceed the legal threshold.

(b) z=(7−8)/1.5=−0.667. P(Z>−0.667)=Φ(0.667)≈0.7475. About 74.75% of tests are positive at the 7 mg/L threshold.

(c) Of all tested subjects: 9.2% exceed 10 mg/L (truly impaired) and 74.75%−9.2%=65.55% test positive but are below 10 mg/L. P(above legal limit | positive test) = 9.2%/74.75% = 12.3%. Only 12.3% of positive roadside tests correspond to legal impairment.

(d) The defence lawyer is substantially correct. The roadside test at 7 mg/L is positive for 75% of the population but legally impaired persons are only 9.2% — meaning 87.7% of positive tests are people below the legal threshold (false positives in the legal sense, though the system may be designed to err on the side of caution). The lawyer can argue that a positive roadside test alone provides very weak evidence of legal impairment. The test serves as a screening tool — intended to flag potential impairment for confirmatory laboratory testing — not as a standalone prosecution basis. A court should require the confirmatory 10 mg/L measurement before conviction.

P(X>10)=9.2% · P(test positive)=74.75% · conditional P(legal|positive)=12.3% · false positive rate in legal screening context · screening vs confirmatory test distinction
T3 · BTwo expert witnesses disagree about a contamination case. Expert A: contamination levels are N(μ=40, σ=5) ppm. Expert B: N(μ=45, σ=8) ppm. The legal limit is 50 ppm. (a) Under each model, what is P(exceeding 50 ppm)? (b) A sample of 200 readings shows 28 readings above 50 ppm. Which model better fits? (c) The observed proportion is 14%. Compute z for each model's predicted probability and state which fits significantly better.

(a) Expert A: z=(50−40)/5=2.00. P(Z>2.00)=1−0.9772=0.0228 (2.28%). Expert B: z=(50−45)/8=0.625. P(Z>0.625)≈1−Φ(0.625)≈1−0.7340=0.2660 (26.6%).

(b) Observed: 28/200=0.14=14%. Expert A predicts 2.28%, Expert B predicts 26.6%. The observed 14% is far from A's prediction (2.28%) and somewhat closer to B's (26.6%), though still not matching B exactly either.

(c) For Expert A: expected exceedances = 200×0.0228=4.56. Observed=28. z=(28−4.56)/√(200×0.0228×0.9772)=23.44/√4.45=23.44/2.11=11.1. Catastrophically far — Model A is comprehensively rejected. For Expert B: expected=200×0.266=53.2. Observed=28. z=(28−53.2)/√(200×0.266×0.734)=−25.2/√39.05=−25.2/6.25=−4.03. Also statistically significant but less extreme. Neither model fits perfectly; B is far less wrong. The true distribution likely lies between A and B — perhaps N(42, 7) or similar. Expert A's model is statistically untenable; Expert B's overpredicts exceedances but is in the right order of magnitude.

T3 · CA judge must interpret a statistical expert's statement: "The defendant's financial transactions were 3.2 standard deviations above the mean for this industry. This is extremely unusual." (a) What is P(Z > 3.2)? (b) If the industry has 10,000 firms, how many would be expected to exceed this level by chance? (c) The defence argues: "With 10,000 firms, seeing one at 3.2σ is not surprising at all." Evaluate this argument quantitatively. (d) Explain the difference between the probability for a single firm and the probability for the most extreme firm in a large sample.

(a) P(Z>3.2)≈1−0.9993=0.0007. About 0.07% — less than 1 in 1000.

(b) Expected firms above 3.2σ: 10,000×0.0007=7 firms.

(c) The defence has a valid statistical point. In an industry of 10,000 firms, we expect approximately 7 firms to exceed 3.2σ purely by chance — without any wrongdoing. Finding one firm at 3.2σ, when 7 are expected, is not statistically surprising at all. In fact, if no firms exceeded 3.2σ in a 10,000-firm industry, that would be the surprising result. The expert's statement about a single transaction being "extremely unusual" conflates the unconditional probability for one entity (0.07%) with the context of large-scale screening (where 7 such entities are expected). This is a multiple testing / base rate argument.

(d) For a single pre-specified firm: P(exceeding 3.2σ) = 0.0007 — genuinely rare. For the most extreme firm in 10,000: P(at least one exceeds 3.2σ) = 1−(1−0.0007)^10,000 ≈ 1−e^(−7) ≈ 0.999 — near certain. When investigators screen thousands of firms and flag the most extreme, the flagging itself carries no probabilistic weight because you are guaranteed to find extreme values in large samples. This is precisely why financial fraud investigations require additional qualitative evidence beyond statistical extremity alone.

PROB 03
Binomial Distribution — Four Characteristics, Formula, and Legal Applications
Language First

The Binomial distribution models the number of successes in a fixed number of independent trials, where each trial has exactly two outcomes (success/failure) and the probability of success is constant.

The four characteristics are essential to memorise — they appear in short questions directly: (1) Fixed number of trials n. (2) Each trial is independent. (3) Exactly two outcomes per trial: success or failure. (4) Probability of success p is constant across all trials.

Legal Applications Are Everywhere

Jury selection (each juror: minority or not), conviction rates (each case: guilty or not), compliance audits (each firm: compliant or not), election fraud detection (each ballot: valid or fraudulent), drug test results (each test: positive or negative). Any legal scenario with repeated binary outcomes under fixed conditions is a Binomial problem.

Mechanics & Notation
Notation: X ~ B(n, p)   n = number of trials, p = probability of success
PMF: P(X=k) = C(n,k) · pᵏ · (1−p)ⁿ⁻ᵏ   for k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n
Combination: C(n,k) = n! / [k!(n−k)!]
Mean: E(X) = μ = np
Variance: Var(X) = σ² = npq   where q = 1−p
Std deviation: σ = √(npq)
P(X ≥ k): = 1 − P(X ≤ k−1) = 1 − [P(X=0)+...+P(X=k−1)]
Normal approximation: valid when np ≥ 5 AND nq ≥ 5
C(n,k) — The Counting Part

C(n,k) counts the number of ways to choose k successes from n trials. It represents all the different orderings of k successes and (n−k) failures. C(5,2) = 5!/(2!3!) = 10 means there are 10 different sequences of exactly 2 successes in 5 trials. The formula then multiplies by pᵏ(1−p)ⁿ⁻ᵏ — the probability of any ONE specific sequence. Key values: C(n,0)=1, C(n,1)=n, C(n,n)=1.

Worked Examples
Easy — Identify the Distribution and Compute P(X=k)

A magistrate court convicts 70% of cases brought before it. In the next 5 cases, find P(exactly 3 convictions).

Check characteristics: n=5 (fixed), independent cases, two outcomes (conviction/acquittal), p=0.70 constant. X ~ B(5, 0.70) ✓

P(X=3): C(5,3) · (0.70)³ · (0.30)² = 10 · 0.343 · 0.09 = 10 × 0.03087 = 0.3087

There is about a 30.9% chance of exactly 3 convictions in the next 5 cases.

Medium — Mean, Variance, and At-Least Probability

A fraud detection system correctly flags fraudulent claims with probability p=0.80. In a batch of 10 claims (all fraudulent), find: (a) E(X) and σ, (b) P(X=10), (c) P(X ≥ 8).

(a) E(X) = np = 10×0.80 = 8 claims expected to be flagged. σ = √(npq) = √(10×0.8×0.2) = √1.6 = 1.265

(b) P(X=10) = C(10,10)·(0.8)¹⁰·(0.2)⁰ = 1·0.1074·1 = 0.1074. There's a 10.7% chance all 10 are caught.

(c) P(X≥8) = P(X=8)+P(X=9)+P(X=10).

P(X=8) = C(10,8)·(0.8)⁸·(0.2)² = 45·0.1678·0.04 = 0.3020

P(X=9) = C(10,9)·(0.8)⁹·(0.2)¹ = 10·0.1342·0.2 = 0.2684

P(X≥8) = 0.3020 + 0.2684 + 0.1074 = 0.6778. About 67.8% chance that 8 or more of the 10 fraudulent claims are caught.

Hard — When the Four Characteristics Are Violated

A lawyer argues: "We can use the Binomial to model the number of biased jurors in a panel, since each juror is either biased or not." Evaluate whether each Binomial assumption holds in a real jury selection scenario, and explain what violations mean for the model's validity.

Characteristic 1 — Fixed n: ✓ Usually holds — jury size is fixed at 12 (or as specified).

Characteristic 2 — Independence: Potentially violated. Jurors deliberate together — social influence can create dependence. If one juror reveals their position in deliberation, others may be influenced. Pre-deliberation independence is plausible but post-deliberation independence is not.

Characteristic 3 — Binary outcomes: ✓ Each juror is biased or not — truly binary for the research question.

Characteristic 4 — Constant p: Potentially violated. The probability of bias may vary systematically by juror background, occupation, race, or age. A retired police officer may have a different probability of bias against a criminal defendant than a social worker. If p varies across jurors, the Binomial assumption fails — a heterogeneous model (different p for each juror) would be more appropriate.

Conclusion: The Binomial is a useful approximation for jury selection at the pool stage (before deliberation, with random selection) but breaks down in deliberation modelling and when juror heterogeneity is relevant. The court should treat Binomial-based statistical arguments as approximate, not exact.

PROB 04
Bayes' Theorem — Updating Beliefs with Evidence and the Prosecutor's Fallacy
Language First

Bayes' Theorem tells you how to update a probability estimate when new evidence arrives. You start with a prior probability P(H) — your belief before seeing evidence. You then observe evidence E and use Bayes' theorem to compute the posterior probability P(H|E) — your updated belief after seeing E.

This is the mathematically rigorous answer to: "Given that the test was positive, what is the actual probability the hypothesis is true?" It corrects for base rates, sensitivity, and false positive rates simultaneously.

The Prosecutor's Fallacy — Most Important Legal Misuse of Probability

The prosecutor's fallacy is confusing P(evidence | innocent) with P(innocent | evidence). A prosecutor might say: "There is only a 1-in-a-million chance this DNA match occurred by chance" — meaning P(match | innocent) = 0.000001. But the relevant question is P(innocent | match). If the suspect was drawn from a pool of 10 million people, we'd expect 10 innocent matches. With 1 guilty person and 10 innocent matches, P(innocent | match) = 10/11 ≈ 91% — most matches are innocent. Bayes' theorem prevents this error.

Mechanics & Notation
Bayes' Theorem: P(H|E) = P(E|H) · P(H) / P(E)
Law of Total Probability: P(E) = P(E|H)·P(H) + P(E|H')·P(H')
Full form: P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / [P(E|H)·P(H) + P(E|H')·P(H')]

P(H): Prior probability — belief before evidence
P(H|E): Posterior probability — updated belief after evidence
P(E|H): Likelihood — how probable is the evidence IF H is true?
P(E|H'): False positive rate — how probable is evidence IF H is false?

Odds form (elegant): Posterior odds = Prior odds × Likelihood Ratio
where: Odds(H) = P(H)/P(H')   LR = P(E|H)/P(E|H')
Worked Examples
Easy — Medical Test: Prior, Likelihood, Posterior

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 95% sensitive (P(+|disease)=0.95) and 95% specific (P(−|no disease)=0.95, so P(+|no disease)=0.05). A person tests positive. What is P(disease | positive test)?

P(H) = 0.01, P(H') = 0.99, P(E|H) = 0.95, P(E|H') = 0.05.

P(E) = 0.95×0.01 + 0.05×0.99 = 0.0095 + 0.0495 = 0.059

P(H|E) = 0.95×0.01 / 0.059 = 0.0095/0.059 = 0.161 = 16.1%

Despite a 95% accurate test, a positive result means only a 16% chance of actually having the disease. The low prior (1%) is what drives this — most positives are false positives from the healthy majority. This counterintuitive result is the base rate neglect problem that affects medical, forensic, and legal evidence interpretation.

Medium — DNA Evidence in Court

A random match probability for a DNA profile is 1 in 100,000 (P(match|innocent)=0.00001). A suspect is picked from a database of 500,000 people; police say the match "proves guilt." The prior probability of any random person in the database being guilty is 1/500,000. Compute the posterior probability of guilt given the match.

P(guilty) = 1/500,000 = 0.000002. P(innocent) = 0.999998.

P(match|guilty) ≈ 1.0 (DNA matches itself). P(match|innocent) = 0.00001.

P(match) = 1.0×0.000002 + 0.00001×0.999998 = 0.000002 + 0.000010 = 0.000012

P(guilty|match) = 1.0×0.000002 / 0.000012 = 0.167 = 16.7%

The "1 in 100,000" match probability does NOT translate to 99.999% certainty of guilt. Given the database search, the posterior probability of guilt is only 16.7%. The prosecutor would commit the prosecutor's fallacy by presenting 1-in-100,000 as near-certain guilt. The correct statement is: "Given this match in a database of 500,000, there is a 16.7% probability the matched person is guilty." Additional corroborating evidence is needed.

Hard — Sequential Updating with Multiple Evidence Items

A regulatory investigation starts with a prior of 10% probability that a firm committed fraud (P(fraud)=0.10). Two independent pieces of evidence are found: Evidence 1 — suspicious transaction pattern, P(E₁|fraud)=0.80, P(E₁|clean)=0.20. Evidence 2 — whistleblower report, P(E₂|fraud)=0.70, P(E₂|clean)=0.10. Update the probability sequentially.

After Evidence 1: P(E₁) = 0.80×0.10 + 0.20×0.90 = 0.08 + 0.18 = 0.26. P(fraud|E₁) = 0.80×0.10/0.26 = 0.08/0.26 = 0.308.

After Evidence 2 (using P(fraud)=0.308 as new prior): P(E₂) = 0.70×0.308 + 0.10×0.692 = 0.2156 + 0.0692 = 0.2848. P(fraud|E₁,E₂) = 0.70×0.308/0.2848 = 0.2156/0.2848 = 0.757 = 75.7%

Starting from 10% prior: after suspicious transactions → 30.8%, after whistleblower report on top → 75.7%. Each piece of evidence substantially updates the probability. At 75.7%, the civil standard (balance of probabilities, >50%) is met but the criminal standard ("beyond reasonable doubt," typically ~95%+) is not. Bayes' theorem makes the evidence-to-conviction pathway explicit and quantifiable.

T3 · AA polygraph (lie detector) has 88% sensitivity (P(+|lying)=0.88) and 86% specificity (P(−|truthful)=0.86). In a suspect pool where 20% are estimated to be lying. (a) Find P(lying|test positive). (b) Find P(truthful|test negative). (c) A government agency proposes mandatory polygraph screening of 10,000 employees, expecting 500 spies. How many true positives, false positives, true negatives, and false negatives are expected? (d) Evaluate the agency's claim that "88% accuracy justifies mandatory screening."

(a) P(L)=0.20, P(+|L)=0.88, P(+|T)=0.14 (false positive rate). P(+)=0.88×0.20+0.14×0.80=0.176+0.112=0.288. P(L|+)=0.176/0.288=0.611=61.1%.

(b) P(−|T)=0.86, P(−|L)=0.12. P(−)=0.12×0.20+0.86×0.80=0.024+0.688=0.712. P(T|−)=0.688/0.712=0.966=96.6%. A negative test strongly suggests truthfulness.

(c) 10,000 employees, 500 spies (P(spy)=0.05), 9,500 non-spies. True positives: 500×0.88=440. False negatives: 500×0.12=60 spies missed. False positives: 9,500×0.14=1,330 innocent employees flagged. True negatives: 9,500×0.86=8,170. Total positives: 440+1,330=1,770. P(spy|+)=440/1770=24.9%.

(d) The "88% accurate" claim is seriously misleading. Among the 1,770 employees who test positive: only 440 (24.9%) are actual spies — 1,330 innocent employees will be falsely flagged. The screening creates 3 false accusations for every 1 true positive. Meanwhile, 60 actual spies escape detection. The policy will destroy 1,330 careers through false accusations while catching 440 of 500 spies. Whether this trade-off is acceptable is a policy question, but the claim that "88% accuracy justifies screening" ignores the base rate (5% prevalence) and the asymmetric harm of false positives vs false negatives in this context.

P(spy|+)=24.9% with 5% prevalence vs 20% prevalence giving 61.1% · 1330 false positives vs 440 true positives · 88% accuracy claim misleading · base rate drives policy consequences
T3 · BDefence of innocence problem: A murder was committed in a city of 800,000 people. A suspect was identified by an eyewitness. Eyewitness identification accuracy is 80% (P(correct ID|truly guilty)=0.80) and the false ID rate is 0.001% per innocent person. (a) Compute P(suspect is guilty | identified by witness). (b) The prosecution adds fingerprint evidence: P(match|guilty)=0.99, P(match|innocent)=0.0002. Update the probability. (c) Is the combined evidence sufficient for "beyond reasonable doubt" (assume ≥95% threshold)? (d) What additional evidence would most efficiently raise the posterior?

(a) Prior P(G)=1/800,000=0.00000125. P(ID|G)=0.80, P(ID|I)=0.00001 (0.001%). P(ID)=0.80×0.00000125+0.00001×0.999999=0.000001+0.00001=0.000011. P(G|ID)=0.80×0.00000125/0.000011=0.000001/0.000011=0.0909=9.1%. Eyewitness alone: 9.1% probability of guilt.

(b) New prior=0.0909. P(FP|G)=0.99, P(FP|I)=0.0002. P(FP)=0.99×0.0909+0.0002×0.9091=0.08999+0.0001818=0.09017. P(G|ID,FP)=0.99×0.0909/0.09017=0.08999/0.09017=0.998=99.8%.

(c) Combined posterior: 99.8% — this exceeds the 95% threshold and would generally meet the "beyond reasonable doubt" standard. The fingerprint evidence has an extremely high likelihood ratio: LR=0.99/0.0002=4,950. This single piece of high-quality forensic evidence transformed 9.1% to 99.8%.

(d) Most efficient additional evidence: any evidence with a very high likelihood ratio. The LR for fingerprints (4,950) was transformative. Additional forensic evidence (CCTV footage, cell tower data, a second independent eyewitness) would each multiply the odds by their respective LR. Evidence that is only slightly more probable under guilt than innocence (LR close to 1 — e.g. character witnesses, motive alone) contributes little. The defence would look for evidence with LR < 1 (more probable under innocence) to reduce the posterior below 95%.

Prior=1/800,000 · P(G|ID)=9.1% · P(G|ID+FP)=99.8% · LR=4,950 for fingerprint · exceeds 95% BRD threshold · LR framework for evidence efficiency
T3 · CA judge receives expert testimony that "the probability of finding this blood stain pattern if the defendant were innocent is 1 in 10,000." The defendant's lawyer counters: "This figure tells you nothing about guilt." (a) Identify what probability is being stated and what probability is needed. (b) Demonstrate using numbers (assume 500,000 people could plausibly be the perpetrator) why the lawyer is substantially correct. (c) What information would need to be added for the expert's evidence to be legally meaningful? (d) If a second independent test also gives 1-in-10,000 for innocence, show the updated posterior — and explain why two weak items of evidence can together constitute strong evidence.

(a) Stated: P(blood pattern | innocent) = 0.0001 — the likelihood of the evidence given innocence. Needed by the court: P(innocent | blood pattern) = the posterior probability of innocence given the evidence. The expert stated a likelihood; the court needs a posterior. These are entirely different probabilities separated by Bayes' theorem and the base rate.

(b) P(G)=1/500,000=0.000002. P(E|G)≈1 (blood pattern present if guilty). P(E|I)=0.0001. P(E)=1×0.000002+0.0001×0.999998≈0.000002+0.0001=0.000102. P(G|E)=0.000002/0.000102=0.0196=1.96%. The lawyer is substantially correct: even with a 1-in-10,000 random match, the posterior probability of guilt is only 1.96%. The expert's testimony, by itself, is almost meaningless without base rate information.

(c) For the evidence to be legally meaningful, the court needs: (i) the prior probability of the suspect's guilt (why was this person a suspect?); (ii) the complete population of possible perpetrators; (iii) the false positive rate (is 1-in-10,000 for this specific type of pattern, or for any blood pattern?). The expert should present a full Bayesian analysis, not just the random match probability.

(d) Two independent tests, each P(Eᵢ|I)=0.0001. Combined P(E₁∩E₂|I)=0.0001²=0.00000001 (if independent). P(E₁∩E₂|G)≈1. Updated: P(G)=0.000002. P(E₁∩E₂)≈0.00000001×0.999998+0.000002≈0.0000000100+0.000002≈0.00000201. P(G|both)≈0.000002/0.00000201=0.995=99.5%. Two pieces of evidence each giving 1-in-10,000 random match probabilities combine (via independent LRs of 10,000 each) for a combined LR of 10,000²=100,000,000 — completely overwhelming the prior. This is why corroborating evidence matters: each independent item multiplies the odds by its LR, and independent evidence stacks multiplicatively rather than additively.

Stated likelihood vs needed posterior · P(G|E)=1.96% despite 1-in-10,000 random match · lawyer substantially correct · needs prior and population size · two independent tests: LR=10,000²=10⁸ → P(G)=99.5% · multiplicative stacking of independent evidence
QR Bar — Quick Reference
Bayes' Formula
P(H|E) = P(E|H)·P(H) / [P(E|H)·P(H) + P(E|H')·P(H')] · Prior × Likelihood → Posterior
Odds Form
Posterior odds = Prior odds × LR · LR = P(E|H)/P(E|H') · P(H)/(1−P(H)) = odds
Prosecutor's Fallacy
P(E|innocent) ≠ P(innocent|E) · Always need base rate · Low match probability ≠ high probability of guilt
Legal Standards as Posteriors
Balance of probabilities: P(H|E) > 0.50 · Beyond reasonable doubt: P(H|E) ≥ ~0.95+ · Both require base rate + evidence
