Point Estimation — Parameters, Statistics, and What a Sample Can Tell You
Language First

A parameter is a fixed (usually unknown) number describing the population: μ (population mean), σ (population standard deviation), p (population proportion). A statistic is computed from a sample and used to estimate the parameter: x̅ estimates μ; p̂ estimates p.

A point estimate is a single number. An interval estimate gives a range. Point estimation is the first step before any confidence interval or hypothesis test.

Legal Translation

A regulator cannot survey every business in the country. A sample of 200 firms produces a point estimate of the violation rate. That estimate is used to justify a policy. The legal question: how reliable is the sample? How representative? Any challenge to the policy can attack the point estimate’s validity.

Mechanics & Notation
x̅ (sample mean) = (x₁ + x₂ + … + xₙ) / n  ··· estimates μ
p̂ (sample proportion) = number of successes / n  ··· estimates p
s² (sample variance) = Σ(xᵢ - x̅)² / (n-1)  ··· estimates σ² (note: n-1 not n for unbiasedness)
SE (standard error of x̅) = σ/√n  ··· measures how far x̅ tends to be from μ
Unbiased estimator: E(θ̂) = θ  ··· on average the estimator hits the true parameter
The One Error to Avoid

Dividing by n instead of n-1 for sample variance gives a biased estimator (it systematically underestimates σ²). In court, using biased estimates can be challenged as incorrect statistical practice. Always use n-1 for the sample variance.

Worked Examples
Easy — Compute x̅ and p̂ from a Small Sample

A regulator samples 6 restaurants and records hygiene scores: 72, 85, 64, 91, 78, 70. An auditor counts 2 restaurants with scores below 75 as non-compliant.

(a) x̅ = (72+85+64+91+78+70)/6 = 460/6 = 76.67. (b) p̂ = 2/6 = 0.333 (33.3% non-compliance rate). These are point estimates of the population mean score and population non-compliance proportion. They are not the parameters — those would require surveying all restaurants.

Medium — Standard Error and What It Means for Policy

A food safety authority surveys n=100 restaurants and finds x̅=74 with σ=12 (assumed known). Compute the SE of x̅ and interpret for a policy brief.

SE = σ/√n = 12/√100 = 12/10 = 1.2. Interpretation: the sample mean of 74 is expected to be about 1.2 points away from the true population mean in a typical sample. If we sampled again, we’d likely get a mean between roughly 71.6 and 76.4 (within 2 SE of 74). The SE quantifies how much sampling variability affects our point estimate. For a policy memo: “Our estimate of the average hygiene score is 74 ± 1.2 (SE), based on 100 restaurants.” A smaller SE makes the estimate more precise — achieved by increasing n.

Hard — Sampling Bias vs Estimation Bias — A Legal Challenge

A company is sued for wage violations. The plaintiff’s expert surveys 50 of the 200 employees working in the main office and computes x̅ = Rs.28,500/month as the average wage, claiming this is below the Rs.30,000 minimum wage. The company argues: (1) the sample is biased — main office employees are not representative of all workers who include field staff; (2) even if unbiased, a sample of 50 from 200 has high variability. Evaluate both challenges.

Challenge 1 — Sampling bias: if field staff earn more than office staff, sampling only office staff produces an estimator that systematically underestimates the overall average. This is selection bias, not statistical estimation bias. E(x̅ᴬᵐᵀᵉᵒ) ≠ μᴬᴯᴸ — the office mean is a biased estimate of the company-wide mean. The plaintiff’s expert should have stratified the sample (proportional representation of office and field workers). A simple random sample from all 200 would be unbiased.

Challenge 2 — Sampling variability: SE = s/√n. If within the office group s≈12,000 then SE=12,000/√50=1,697. A 95% CI for the office mean would be roughly 28,500±3,394 = (25,106 to 31,894). The interval includes Rs.30,000 — the estimate is imprecise enough that the true mean could be above the minimum. The company’s variability challenge is valid. The plaintiff’s case requires either a larger sample or a sample from all 200 employees.

T2 · Bn=64 cases, x̅=Rs.85,000 settlement, s=Rs.24,000. Compute SE(x̅). If n doubled to 128, what would the new SE be? What does this illustrate about sample size and precision?

SE = s/√n = 24,000/√64 = 24,000/8 = Rs.3,000. At n=128: SE = 24,000/√128 = 24,000/11.31 = Rs.2,121. Reduction: 3,000-2,121=Rs.879 (29% reduction in SE).

T2 · Cp̂ = 14/80 in a survey of 80 court hearings found to start late. Compute p̂, SE(p̂), and interpret for a court administration report.

p̂ = 14/80 = 0.175 (17.5% late start rate). SE(p̂) = √[p̂(1-p̂)/n] = √[0.175(0.825)/80] = √[0.1444/80] = √0.001805 = 0.0425.

Administration report: “In a sample of 80 hearings, an estimated 17.5% started late (SE = 4.25 percentage points). The true late-start rate is unlikely to be below 9% or above 26% under reasonable assumptions.” SE of 4.25 points means the estimate has moderate precision — useful for identifying a problem but not precise enough to claim, for instance, that the rate is above or below 20% with confidence.

T2 · DTwo sampling schemes to estimate the proportion of defective products in a shipment of 5,000 units. Scheme A: randomly select 100 units. Scheme B: select every 50th unit (systematic sample). A quality inspector challenges Scheme B as potentially biased. Identify when Scheme B would be biased and when it would be effectively equivalent to Scheme A.

Scheme B (systematic) is equivalent to Scheme A (simple random) when there is no periodic pattern in the production line that aligns with the interval of 50. If defects occur randomly, every 50th unit is effectively random. Scheme B is BIASED when production has a periodic defect pattern with period 50 or a divisor of 50 (e.g. every 25th, 10th, or 50th item off a machine is defective due to a maintenance cycle). If the production machine is calibrated every 50 units and degrades between calibrations, every 50th item would be systematically different from the others.

Legal implication: a company defending product quality that used systematic sampling should demonstrate no periodic production defect pattern. If the plaintiff can show that the production process has a periodicity that matches or divides the sampling interval, the systematic sample is biased and the point estimate p̂ cannot be trusted as an unbiased estimate of the true defect proportion.

Systematic sampling as effectively random (no periodic pattern) vs biased (periodic defects) · production periodicity as the legal challenge · legal burden to demonstrate absence of systematic pattern
T3 · AAn expert witness claims: “Our survey of 30 employees shows a mean of Rs.26,000/month, well below the Rs.30,000 minimum. This proves the employer is violating the minimum wage law.” The defence expert responds: “A sample of 30 cannot prove anything about 3,000 employees.” (a) What does x̅=Rs.26,000 from n=30 actually establish? (b) Is the defence expert’s response correct? (c) What would the plaintiff need to strengthen the point estimate into genuine evidence of a violation? (d) What is the difference between a statistical estimate and legal proof?

(a) x̅=Rs.26,000 is a point estimate of the population mean wage. It establishes that in this particular sample of 30, the average wage was Rs.26,000. It suggests the population mean may be below Rs.30,000 — but with n=30, the standard error is substantial (if s≈8,000, SE=1,461; if s≈14,000, SE=2,556).

(b) Defence expert’s response: partially correct in spirit but overstated. A sample of 30 CAN constitute statistical evidence — sampling is the standard method for population inference. The defence is correct that 30 cannot definitively “prove” a violation across 3,000 employees, but they are wrong to say it “cannot establish anything.” It establishes a prima facie statistical case that the mean is below Rs.30,000 in the population, subject to confidence intervals.

(c) To strengthen: (i) compute a 95% CI for μ — if the ENTIRE interval lies below Rs.30,000, the statistical case is stronger; (ii) increase n — n=30 is the minimum, but n=200+ would provide much tighter estimates; (iii) ensure the sample is representative (stratified by department/role); (iv) provide individual wage records — point estimates are unnecessary if complete payroll data is available.

(d) Statistical estimate vs legal proof: a point estimate tells you what the sample suggests about the population. Legal proof requires meeting a standard of evidence (balance of probabilities in civil cases; beyond reasonable doubt in criminal). A point estimate below Rs.30,000 is consistent with a violation but does not meet any legal standard alone. Combined with a CI that excludes Rs.30,000, the evidence becomes substantially stronger. Complete payroll records are always legally superior to estimates.

Point estimate as prima facie evidence not proof · SE quantifies uncertainty · CI that excludes Rs.30,000 is stronger evidence · sample size matters for precision · difference between statistical inference and legal proof standard
T3 · BA government survey estimates the tax compliance rate at p̂=0.72 from n=400. A minister announces: “72% of taxpayers comply.” Three years later, with n=900, p̂=0.69. The minister claims: “Compliance has fallen by 3 percentage points.” (a) Compute SE for both estimates. (b) Is the 3-point fall statistically meaningful? (c) What test would formally evaluate whether there has been a real change? (d) The opposition claims this is evidence of failed tax policy. Evaluate the strength of this claim.

(a) SE₁ = √[0.72(0.28)/400] = √[0.2016/400] = √0.000504 = 0.0224. SE₂ = √[0.69(0.31)/900] = √[0.2139/900] = √0.0002377 = 0.0154.

(b) The 3-point fall: the SE of the difference is √(SE₁²+SE₂²) = √(0.000504+0.0002377) = √0.0007417 = 0.0272. The observed difference is 0.03/0.0272 = 1.1 standard errors. This is less than 1.96 (the 95% threshold) — the difference is NOT statistically significant at the 5% level. The 3-point fall is within sampling variability.

(c) Formal test: a two-proportion z-test. H₀: p₁=p₂ (no change). H₁: p₁>p₂ (compliance fell). Test statistic z = (0.72-0.69)/0.0272 = 1.1. p-value ≈ 0.136 (one-tailed). Fail to reject H₀ at α=0.05.

(d) Opposition claim: statistically, the claim of “failed policy” is not supported by this data — the observed fall is within sampling error. The opposition cannot distinguish between a genuine 3-point fall and natural sampling variation. A stronger claim would require larger samples or multiple consistent measurements showing the same direction. Three years of data from two samples is insufficient to establish a trend. The minister’s announcement of “72%” was also overly precise; “72% ± 4.4%” would have been more defensible and would have made the later 69% appear well within the original estimate’s margin of error.

SE of proportion · SE of difference between two proportions · z=1.1 not significant at 0.05 · two-proportion z-test for formal comparison · minister overstatement by not reporting SE · statistical claim vs political claim distinction
T3 · CDuring a class-action wage theft case, the plaintiff’s statistical expert proposes estimating the average underpayment from a random sample of 150 employees out of 2,400. The company objects on the grounds that “individual calculations must be made for each employee.” The judge must decide whether statistical sampling evidence is admissible. (a) What standard justifies using a sample to estimate total damages? (b) How should the expert design the sample to maximise its defensibility? (c) If x̅=Rs.4,200/month underpayment (s=Rs.2,800, n=150), compute the 95% CI for μ and the 95% CI for total underpayment across 2,400 employees. (d) What does the company need to show to defeat the sampling evidence?

(a) Statistical sampling is well-established in class-action litigation. The standard is that the sample must be representative, the statistical methodology must be sound, and the estimator must have quantifiable precision (confidence interval). Many jurisdictions permit sampling in wage class-actions when individual calculation is impractical. The legal foundation: statistical inference is a recognised tool for establishing facts about large populations from representative samples.

(b) Defensible sample design: (i) Simple random sample from the full 2,400-employee list (not convenience or stratified by department without justification); (ii) n=150 selected by certified random number generation with documented seed; (iii) Stratify by job type and tenure if these affect underpayment rates (ensures representation); (iv) All 150 must have actual wage records reviewed, not estimated; (v) Expert documents the sampling protocol in advance (pre-registration).

(c) CI for μ: x̅ ± t*(s/√n) ≈ 4,200 ± 1.976(2,800/√150) = 4,200 ± 1.976(228.6) = 4,200 ± 451.6 = (Rs.3,748 to Rs.4,652). CI for total: multiply bounds by 2,400: 2,400×3,748 = Rs.8,995,200 to 2,400×4,652 = Rs.11,164,800. The court would award total damages in the range Rs.9.0M to Rs.11.2M with 95% confidence.

(d) To defeat sampling evidence: (i) Show the sample is non-random (selected by plaintiff’s expert in a biased way); (ii) Show the 150 employees are not representative of the 2,400 (e.g. primarily from one department); (iii) Provide full payroll records for all 2,400 — if complete data is available, sampling evidence becomes unnecessary and the company can contest each record individually; (iv) Challenge the expert’s methodology: did they account for legitimate differences (hours worked, overtime, bonuses)?

Sampling admissibility in class-action · representativeness as legal standard · t-interval for μ (n=150, use t not z exactly) · total damages = 2,400 × CI bounds · four ways to defeat sampling evidence · complete payroll records superior to estimates

Confidence Intervals for Means — z-Interval, t-Interval, and Width Analysis
Language First

A confidence interval (CI) gives a range of plausible values for a parameter. “95% CI” means: if we repeated this sampling procedure many times, 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true parameter. It does NOT mean “there is a 95% probability that μ is in this specific interval” — μ is fixed.

Two versions: z-interval when σ is known; t-interval when σ is unknown (the common case).

The Most Common CI Misinterpretation in Court

“We are 95% confident that the true mean is Rs.45,000” does NOT mean there is a 95% probability the parameter is Rs.45,000. A specific interval either contains μ or it doesn’t. The 95% refers to the long-run procedure, not this specific interval. Expert witnesses and judges frequently misstate this — know the correct interpretation.

Mechanics & Notation
z-interval (known σ): x̅ ± z* × (σ/√n)
t-interval (unknown σ): x̅ ± t* × (s/√n)  ··· df = n-1
Common z* values: 90% → 1.645 · 95% → 1.96 · 99% → 2.576
Margin of error (ME): z* × (σ/√n) · Half-width of the interval
Width factors: Higher confidence → wider · Larger n → narrower · Larger σ → wider
Required n for given ME: n ≥ (z* × σ / ME)²
z vs t Decision

Use z when σ is known (rare in practice). Use t when σ is unknown (almost always). When n>30, t≈z so either is acceptable for exam purposes — but know the principle.

Worked Examples
Easy — Compute a 95% z-Interval

x̅=Rs.52,000, σ=Rs.8,000 (known), n=64. Compute the 95% CI.

ME = 1.96 × (8,000/√64) = 1.96 × 1,000 = Rs.1,960. CI: (52,000-1,960, 52,000+1,960) = (Rs.50,040 to Rs.53,960). Interpretation: we are 95% confident this interval captures the true population mean settlement value. The interval is Rs.3,920 wide.

Medium — Comparing 90%, 95%, and 99% Intervals for the Same Data

A labour court receives expert testimony: x̅=Rs.28,500, s=Rs.6,400, n=100. Compute 90%, 95%, and 99% CIs. The minimum wage is Rs.30,000. Which CI(s) include Rs.30,000?

SE = 6,400/√100 = 640. 90%: 28,500 ± 1.645(640) = 28,500 ± 1,053 = (Rs.27,447 to Rs.29,553). 95%: 28,500 ± 1.96(640) = 28,500 ± 1,254 = (Rs.27,246 to Rs.29,754). 99%: 28,500 ± 2.576(640) = 28,500 ± 1,649 = (Rs.26,851 to Rs.30,149).

Only the 99% CI includes Rs.30,000. At 90% and 95% confidence, the entire interval is below Rs.30,000 — providing evidence of a violation. At 99% confidence, the interval barely includes Rs.30,000, giving the employer a stronger argument for reasonable doubt. The choice of confidence level matters for the legal conclusion. A cautious plaintiff would show the 95% CI; a cautious employer would demand 99%.

Hard — Required Sample Size, Width Trade-off, and What to Do When You Don’t Have σ

A regulatory body wants to estimate the average fine paid by businesses with ME ≤ Rs.500 at 95% confidence. A pilot sample of 20 gives s=Rs.3,200. (a) Estimate required n. (b) The survey is constrained to n=150 by budget. What ME will be achieved? (c) At n=150, compute the 95% t-interval given x̅=Rs.4,800, s=Rs.3,200. (d) The opposition argues: “You used a pilot sample for σ — this is circular.” Evaluate.

(a) n ≥ (z*s/ME)² = (1.96×3200/500)² = (12.544)² = 157.4. Need n=158.

(b) At n=150: ME = 1.96×(3200/√150) = 1.96×261.3 = Rs.512. Slightly above target — n=150 gives ME=Rs.512 instead of Rs.500.

(c) t-interval with n=150, df=149: t*≈1.976 (use 1.96 for exam). x̅±t*(s/√n) = 4,800±1.976(3200/√150) = 4,800±516 = (Rs.4,284 to Rs.5,316).

(d) Pilot sample for σ: standard and defensible. The purpose of a pilot sample is precisely to estimate σ when it is unknown in advance. Using a pilot s does not make the inference circular — the MAIN SAMPLE (n=150) provides x̅, and the pilot only informed the sample size calculation. The final interval uses s from the main sample, not the pilot. This is accepted statistical practice. The opposition’s objection conflates two distinct uses of the data.

T2 · BA CI for average case resolution time is (18.4, 23.6) days. (a) What is x̅? (b) What is the ME? (c) If this was a 95% CI with n=49 and the SE can be computed, what was s? (d) How would the interval change if n doubled to 98?

(a) x̅ = (18.4+23.6)/2 = 21 days (midpoint). (b) ME = (23.6-18.4)/2 = 2.6 days. (c) ME = z*×SE: 2.6 = 1.96×(s/7). s/7 = 1.327. s = 9.29 days. (d) At n=98: ME = 1.96×(9.29/√98) = 1.96×0.938 = 1.84 days. New CI: 21±1.84 = (19.16 to 22.84). Width falls from 5.2 to 3.68 days (29% narrower, consistent with 1/√2 factor).

T2 · CTwo damage expert witnesses. Expert A: x̅=Rs.120,000, 95% CI=(Rs.105,000, Rs.135,000), n=100. Expert B: x̅=Rs.132,000, 95% CI=(Rs.108,000, Rs.156,000), n=25. (a) Which estimate is more precise? (b) Do the two CIs overlap? (c) Can both experts be correct simultaneously? (d) Which should the court give more weight to?

(a) Expert A is more precise: ME=Rs.15,000 vs Expert B ME=Rs.24,000. Larger n gives narrower CI. (b) Overlap check: A’s interval is (105,000–135,000); B’s is (108,000–156,000). They overlap between Rs.108,000 and Rs.135,000. (c) Both CIs can simultaneously contain the true μ — a single value in the overlap region (e.g. Rs.120,000) is consistent with both. Overlapping CIs do not mean contradiction. (d) Expert A deserves more weight: same point estimate methodology but 4x larger sample gives 2x narrower CI. Expert B’s wide interval (Rs.48,000 wide) makes any conclusion from it less reliable. However, the court should note that B’s point estimate is Rs.12,000 higher — they would need to understand the sampling difference to explain this.

T2 · DA compliance audit finds that 90% CI for average overtime hours is (52.1, 57.9) hours/week. The legal limit is 55 hours. A lawyer argues: “The mean could be 55 or below — we can’t be sure there is a violation.” Evaluate this argument.

x̅ = (52.1+57.9)/2 = 55. ME = (57.9-52.1)/2 = 2.9. At 90% confidence, the interval (52.1, 57.9) contains 55 exactly at its midpoint. The sample mean is 55 hours exactly — right at the legal limit.

Lawyer’s argument: technically correct. The 90% CI includes values both above and below 55. With the MEAN at exactly 55, neither side has a clear statistical advantage. However: (i) if more than half of individual employees exceed 55 hours, total liability exists even if the mean is 55; (ii) the CI does NOT tell us the proportion above 55 (mean ≠ proportion of individual violations); (iii) a one-sided test H₁: μ>55 with x̅=55 gives a p-value of exactly 0.5 (no evidence). The legal analysis requires individual hour records, not just the mean. The lawyer’s point is valid but incomplete — the mean being at the limit does not establish compliance if individual violations exist.

x̅ at limit − not a violation of the mean criterion · mean at limit gives p-value=0.5 · individual violations vs mean violation · CI covers both above and below limit
T3 · AAn expert witness presents a 99% CI for the average contamination level in a food batch: (38.2, 61.8 mg/kg). The regulatory limit is 50 mg/kg. The prosecution says: “The interval includes 50, proving contamination is possible.” The defence says: “The interval includes 50, proving the contamination could be within safe limits.” (a) Who is interpreting the CI correctly? (b) What does the CI actually establish for the purpose of the prosecution? (c) What additional analysis would definitively establish a violation?

(a) Both are interpreting it in a self-serving but technically defensible way — a CI that includes the threshold is genuinely ambiguous. However, both are making a conceptual error: the CI estimates where μ lies, not whether μ exceeds the limit with certainty. x̅ = (38.2+61.8)/2 = 50 exactly. The sample mean is right at the limit.

(b) What the CI establishes: at 99% confidence, μ is plausibly anywhere between 38.2 and 61.8. The prosecution cannot establish with 99% confidence that μ>50 because the lower bound is below 50. For a hypothesis test H₀:μ=50, H₁:μ>50 at α=0.01: with x̅=50, the test statistic is 0 and the p-value is 0.5. The CI correctly reflects that there is no statistical evidence of a violation.

(c) Definitive analysis: (i) Test each batch individually — the mean being at 50 is insufficient if some batches are systematically above; (ii) One-sided 99% CI: upper bound only matters for the prosecution — a one-sided interval gives a different bound; (iii) Individual unit testing: if n individual units are each tested and most exceed 50, the statistical inference about the mean is unnecessary; (iv) A larger sample to narrow the CI: with the 99% CI being 23.6 units wide, the current n may be too small to resolve the question.

x̅ at limit means p-value=0.5 in one-sided test · both sides technically defensible when CI straddles threshold · prosecution needs CI entirely above 50 for 99% confidence of violation · individual testing superior to mean testing for contamination
T3 · BIn a wrongful termination case, the court must determine whether the average performance rating for terminated employees was statistically different from the company-wide average of 72 (the claimed minimum threshold). A sample of 40 terminated employees gives x̅=68, s=9. (a) Compute the 95% CI and determine if 72 is in the interval. (b) Compute the t-statistic and p-value (approx) for H₀:μ=72. (c) The company argues: “The difference of 4 points is small in absolute terms.” Evaluate this argument statistically and legally. (d) What does it mean for the case if we find a statistically significant result?

(a) SE=9/√40=1.423. t*≈2.023 for df=39. CI: 68±2.023×1.423 = 68±2.879 = (65.12 to 70.88). The threshold 72 is NOT in the 95% CI — statistical evidence that the terminated employees’ true average performance was below 72.

(b) t = (68-72)/1.423 = -4/1.423 = -2.81. For df=39, two-tailed p-value from t=-2.81: p≈0.008. Significant at α=0.05 and α=0.01. (c) Company’s ‘4 points is small’ argument: statistically, 4 points is not small relative to the SE of 1.42 — it is 2.81 SEs below the threshold, which is highly significant. The absolute magnitude of a difference is only meaningful relative to its variability. A 4-point difference with SE=1.42 is more significant than a 20-point difference with SE=15. The company is confusing effect size with p-value. (d) Statistical significance here means: if the true mean performance of terminated employees was 72 (as required), obtaining a sample mean of 68 or lower would happen less than 1% of the time by chance. This is strong statistical evidence that the terminated group’s ratings were systematically below 72. It does not prove individual ratings were falsified, but it supports an inference of discriminatory or improper application of the performance threshold.

95% CI excludes 72 · t-statistic = (x̅-μ₀)/SE · p≈0.008 significant at α=0.01 · absolute difference vs SE · statistical significance as inference about discriminatory pattern
T3 · CA class-action lawsuit claims a pharmaceutical company underreported drug side effects. The plaintiff’s expert computes a 95% CI for the mean adverse event rate per 1,000 patients: (3.8, 7.2). The company’s pre-approval clinical trial reported a rate of 3.2 per 1,000 (population value, not an estimate). (a) Does the CI include 3.2? (b) Compute the t-statistic and p-value for H₀:μ=3.2 given the CI implies x̅=5.5, n=80, s≈4.2. LEC 27
Confidence Intervals for Proportions — p̂ ± z*SE and Sample Size Planning
Language First

When the variable of interest is categorical (compliant/non-compliant, guilty/not guilty, defective/good), we estimate the population proportion p using the sample proportion p̂.

The CI for p is built using the normal approximation to the binomial distribution — valid when np̂ ≥ 5 AND n(1-p̂) ≥ 5. For small samples or extreme proportions (p̂ near 0 or 1), use exact methods instead.

Legal Translation

“17% of surveyed workers reported wage theft” is a sample proportion. The CI around it tells you the plausible range for the true proportion across all workers. A regulator using p̂ to justify a policy intervention must ensure the CI’s lower bound is still above the intervention threshold.

Mechanics & Notation
p̂ = x/n (number of successes / sample size)
95% CI for p: p̂ ± z* √[p̂(1-p̂)/n]
Margin of error: ME = z* √[p̂(1-p̂)/n]
Required n (conservative): n ≥ (z*/ME)² × (0.25)  ··· uses p̂=0.5 (maximum variance)
Validity check: np̂ ≥ 5 AND n(1-p̂) ≥ 5
Why 0.5 for Conservative n?

p̂(1-p̂) is maximised when p̂=0.5, giving 0.25. Using 0.25 in the required-n formula guarantees the ME target is met regardless of the true p. If you have a prior estimate of p, you can use it for a smaller required n.

Worked Examples
Easy — Compute a 95% CI for a Proportion

In a survey of 200 contract disputes, 58 were found to involve ambiguous terms. Compute the 95% CI for the proportion of contract disputes with ambiguous terms.

p̂ = 58/200 = 0.29. Check: np̂=58≥5 ✓, n(1-p̂)=142≥5 ✓. SE = √[0.29×0.71/200] = √[0.2059/200] = √0.001030 = 0.0321. ME = 1.96×0.0321 = 0.0629. CI: (0.29-0.063, 0.29+0.063) = (0.227 to 0.353) or 22.7% to 35.3%. We are 95% confident that between 22.7% and 35.3% of all contract disputes involve ambiguous terms.

Medium — Required Sample Size for a Discrimination Survey

A civil rights organisation plans to survey employees about discriminatory practices. They want ME ≤ 3 percentage points (0.03) at 95% confidence. (a) Use the conservative estimate. (b) If prior research suggests p ≈ 0.15, what n is needed?

(a) Conservative: n ≥ (1.96/0.03)² × 0.25 = (65.33)² × 0.25 = 4268.4 × 0.25 = 1067.1. Need n=1,068.

(b) With p≈ 0.15: n ≥ (1.96/0.03)² × 0.15×0.85 = 4268.4 × 0.1275 = 544.2. Need n=545 — roughly half the conservative sample. If the prior estimate is credible, it saves the organisation significant survey cost. Legal note: if p is unknown or the prior estimate is contested, courts may prefer the conservative approach to avoid undersampling.

Hard — Two Surveys, Different Results — Are They Statistically Consistent?

Survey 1 (plaintiff): n=300, p̂₁=0.38 (38% of employees experienced harassment). Survey 2 (defendant): n=150, p̂₂=0.26 (26%). Both 95% CIs. Do the surveys give statistically inconsistent results?

CI₁: 0.38 ± 1.96√[0.38×0.62/300] = 0.38 ± 1.96×0.0280 = 0.38 ± 0.0549 = (0.325 to 0.435). CI₂: 0.26 ± 1.96√[0.26×0.74/150] = 0.26 ± 1.96×0.0358 = 0.26 ± 0.0701 = (0.190 to 0.330).

The CIs barely overlap (0.325 to 0.330 is the overlap region). This is marginal overlap — suggesting the surveys may be statistically inconsistent, though not conclusively so at 95% confidence. Formal two-proportion z-test: SEᴰᴵᶠᶠ = √[SE₁²+SE₂²] = √[0.000784+0.001281] = 0.0454. z = (0.38-0.26)/0.0454 = 0.12/0.0454 = 2.64. p-value≈0.008 (two-tailed). The surveys ARE statistically different at the 5% level. Most likely explanation: the two surveys sampled different populations (perhaps survey 1 targeted specific departments where harassment was more common, or different time periods). The court must evaluate the sampling methodology of each survey rather than simply averaging the two proportions.

LEC 28
Hypothesis Testing Framework — H₀, H₁, α, p-Values, and Error Types
Language First

Hypothesis testing is a formal decision procedure. We start with a null hypothesis H₀ (the default claim, often equality or “no effect”) and an alternative hypothesis H₁ (what we’re trying to show). We collect data and ask: is the data too extreme to be consistent with H₀?

The p-value is the probability of observing data as extreme as (or more extreme than) ours, IF H₀ were true. Small p-value = strong evidence against H₀.

Legal Translation

H₀ = “defendant is innocent” (default position). H₁ = “defendant is guilty.” Evidence is the data. The standard of proof sets α — how extreme must evidence be before we reject innocence? “Beyond reasonable doubt” = very small α (perhaps 1%). “Balance of probabilities” = larger α (perhaps 5-10%). Hypothesis testing makes this threshold explicit and quantifiable.

Mechanics & Notation
H₀: null hypothesis (no effect, no change, equality) — always contains = or ≤ or ≥
H₁: alternative hypothesis (what we want to show) — contains ≠, <, or >
α: significance level = P(Type I error) = P(reject H₀ | H₀ true). Common: 0.05, 0.01
p-value: P(data this extreme or more | H₀ true). If p ≤ α: reject H₀
Type I error (α): reject H₀ when it is true (false positive — wrongly convict innocent)
Type II error (β): fail to reject H₀ when it is false (false negative — wrongly acquit guilty)
Power = 1-β: probability of correctly rejecting a false H₀
Two-tailed: H₁: μ≠μ₀ — critical region both tails. One-tailed: H₁: μ>μ₀ or μ<μ₀ — one tail
One Error Cannot Be Reduced Without Increasing the Other

If you reduce α (fewer false convictions), you increase β (more false acquittals). The only way to reduce both simultaneously is to increase the sample size n. This trade-off is why legal standards differ by context: criminal law accepts more false acquittals (β) to reduce false convictions (α).

Worked Examples
Easy — Identify H₀ and H₁, Name the Errors

A factory claims its mean noise level is 80 dB or below (the legal limit). A regulator measures noise levels and wants to test whether the factory exceeds the limit.

H₀: μ ≤ 80 dB (factory is within legal limit — default position, no violation). H₁: μ > 80 dB (factory exceeds the limit — one-tailed, the regulator is trying to show exceedance). Type I error: conclude the factory violates the limit when it actually doesn’t (false enforcement action — unfair to the factory). Type II error: fail to detect that the factory exceeds the limit when it actually does (false clearance — harmful to nearby residents).

Which error is more serious? Depends on context. Regulators typically worry more about Type II (missing actual violations) but set α=0.05 to avoid excess false enforcement. A community group might argue for higher power (lower β) even at the cost of some Type I errors.

Medium — p-Value Interpretation in a Wage Dispute

H₀: μ = Rs.30,000 (wages at minimum). H₁: μ < Rs.30,000 (wages below minimum). From a sample of 50 workers, x̅ = Rs.28,800, s = Rs.4,200, t = -2.02. The p-value (one-tailed) = 0.024. Interpret the result at α = 0.05 and at α = 0.01.

At α=0.05: p=0.024 ≤ 0.05. Reject H₀. Conclusion: statistically significant evidence at the 5% level that the mean wage is below Rs.30,000. At α=0.01: p=0.024 > 0.01. Fail to reject H₀. Conclusion: not statistically significant at the 1% level. The result is “significant at 5% but not at 1%.” Legal implication: the strength of the statistical evidence depends on the threshold chosen. A plaintiff’s lawyer would argue for α=0.05 (conventional social science standard); a defence lawyer would argue for α=0.01 (higher bar). The court must understand that significance level is a human choice, not a physical law.

Hard — Designing a Full Hypothesis Test — Setting α, Justifying the Tail, Power Analysis

A pharmaceutical regulator wants to test whether a new drug reduces mean blood pressure by more than 10 mmHg (the minimum clinically significant reduction). From prior studies, σ=15 mmHg. The trial will use n=100 patients. Design the test including: H₀/H₁, α choice with justification, critical value, and power at μ=15 mmHg reduction.

Hypotheses: H₀: μ ≤ 10 mmHg (drug is not clinically effective). H₁: μ > 10 mmHg (drug achieves clinically significant reduction — one-tailed, testing for efficacy).

α=0.05: justified because this is an initial efficacy trial, not a safety trial. A Type I error (approving an ineffective drug) is costly but less harmful than a Type II error (blocking an effective drug). If this were a safety test for side effects, α=0.01 would be more appropriate.

Critical value: z*=1.645 (one-tailed at 0.05). SE=15/√100=1.5. Critical x̅: 10+1.645×1.5=10+2.47=12.47 mmHg. Reject H₀ if x̅>12.47.

Power at μ=15: If true μ=15, what is P(reject H₀)? z=(12.47-15)/1.5=-2.53/1.5=-1.69. Power=P(Z>-1.69)=0.955. 95.5% power — excellent. If the drug truly reduces blood pressure by 15 mmHg, the trial will detect it 95.5% of the time.

One-Sample Z-Test and T-Test — Five-Step Protocol and Legal Applications
Language First

The z-test and t-test are the workhorses of hypothesis testing for means. The logic is: compute how many standard errors the sample mean is from the null value. If that distance is large enough (beyond the critical value), reject H₀.

Use z-test when σ is known. Use t-test when σ is unknown (the common case in practice). For n>30, the difference is small.

Five-Step Protocol — Never Skip a Step

Step 1: State H₀ and H₁. Step 2: Choose α and determine test direction. Step 3: Compute the test statistic. Step 4: Find the critical value or p-value. Step 5: State the conclusion in the context of the problem. Missing the context in Step 5 is the most common exam error.

Mechanics & Notation
z-test statistic: z = (x̅ - μ₀) / (σ/√n)
t-test statistic: t = (x̅ - μ₀) / (s/√n)  ··· df = n-1
Decision rule: reject H₀ if |z| > z* (two-tailed) or z > z* (right) or z < -z* (left)
95% two-tailed: z*=1.96  ··· 95% one-tailed: z*=1.645
99% two-tailed: z*=2.576  ··· 99% one-tailed: z*=2.326
p-value (z=2.00, two-tailed): ≈0.046  ··· p-value (z=2.58): ≈0.010
Worked Examples
Easy — Five-Step z-Test from Start to Finish

A company claims its mean order processing time is 48 hours. An auditor samples 81 orders and finds x̅=50.4 hours, σ=10.8 (known). Test at α=0.05 (two-tailed).

Step 1. H₀: μ=48. H₁: μ≠48. Step 2. α=0.05, two-tailed, z*=1.96. Step 3. z = (50.4-48)/(10.8/√81) = 2.4/1.2 = 2.00. Step 4. |z|=2.00>1.96. Reject H₀. p-value≈0.046<0.05. Step 5. At the 5% significance level, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that the true mean processing time differs from 48 hours. The sample mean of 50.4 hours is significantly higher than the claimed 48, suggesting the company’s claim may be inaccurate.

Medium — t-Test for Wage Compliance

The minimum wage is Rs.30,000/month. A sample of 25 employees gives x̅=Rs.28,600, s=Rs.3,500. Test H₀:μ≥30,000 vs H₁:μ<30,000 at α=0.05.

SE=3500/√25=700. t=(28,600-30,000)/700=-1400/700=-2.00. df=24. Critical t*=-1.711 (one-tailed left). Since t=-2.00<-1.711, reject H₀.

p-value: for t=-2.00 with df=24, p≈0.028. Since 0.028<0.05, reject H₀. Conclusion: at the 5% level, there is significant evidence that the mean wage is below Rs.30,000. This supports a wage violation claim. Note: we used t (not z) because σ is unknown and estimated by s=3,500.

Hard — Two-Sample t-Test — Testing for Discriminatory Wage Gap

A discrimination case compares wages between two groups. Group 1 (minority, n₁=30): x̅₁=Rs.42,000, s₁=Rs.6,000. Group 2 (majority, n₂=40): x̅₂=Rs.47,500, s₂=Rs.7,200. Test H₀:μ₁=μ₂ vs H₁:μ₁≠μ₂ at α=0.05. Assume unequal variances (Welch test).

SEᴰᴵᶠᶠ = √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂) = √(36,000,000/30 + 51,840,000/40) = √(1,200,000 + 1,296,000) = √2,496,000 = 1,580. t = (42,000-47,500)/1,580 = -5,500/1,580 = -3.48.

df ≈ min(n₁-1, n₂-1) = 29 (conservative). Critical t* ≈ ±2.045 for df=29 at α=0.05 two-tailed. |t|=3.48>2.045. Reject H₀.

p-value<0.01 (t=3.48 with df=29 is highly significant). Conclusion: at the 5% level (and also at 1%), there is statistically significant evidence of a wage difference between the two groups. The Rs.5,500/month gap is not attributable to sampling variation alone. This supports the discrimination claim statistically, though establishing causation requires evidence about job roles, performance, and seniority.

Chi-Square Goodness of Fit — Does the Data Match the Claimed Distribution?
Language First

The chi-square (χ²) goodness-of-fit test asks: does the observed distribution of a categorical variable match an expected (theoretical or claimed) distribution? We compare observed counts (O) to expected counts (E) and measure how far they diverge.

A large χ² means the observed data is far from the expected — evidence against the claimed distribution. A small χ² means the data fits the claimed distribution well.

Legal Translation

Classic legal application: jury composition. If the population is 30% from a minority group but only 8% of jury pools are from that group, is the discrepancy due to chance or systematic exclusion? Chi-square goodness-of-fit tests this directly. If χ² is large enough, the difference cannot be attributed to random selection.

Mechanics & Notation
H₀: observed distribution matches expected distribution
H₁: distribution does not match
Test statistic: χ² = Σ (Oᵢ - Eᵢ)² / Eᵢ  ··· summed over all categories
Expected counts: Eᵢ = n × pᵢ (sample size × claimed proportion for category i)
Degrees of freedom: df = k - 1 (k = number of categories)
Validity: all Eᵢ ≥ 5 (merge small categories if needed)
Decision: reject H₀ if χ² > χ²ᶜʳᴵᵗᴵᶜᵃᴸ (from table at df and α)
Selected critical values (α=0.05):
df=1: χ²*=3.841  ··· df=2: 5.991  ··· df=3: 7.815  ··· df=4: 9.488
df=5: 11.070  ··· df=9: 16.919  ··· df=19: 30.144
(α=0.01): df=1: 6.635 · df=2: 9.210 · df=3: 11.345 · df=4: 13.277

Chi-Square Test of Independence — Contingency Tables and Association
Language First

The chi-square test of independence asks: are two categorical variables related, or are they independent? We use a contingency table (rows = categories of variable 1, columns = categories of variable 2) to compare observed joint frequencies to what we’d expect if the variables were independent.

Independence means: knowing one variable tells you nothing about the other. Dependence (association) means the distribution of one variable differs across categories of the other.

Legal Translation

Is the probability of being convicted independent of race? Is the probability of receiving a large fine independent of firm size? Is promotion outcome independent of gender? These are questions about independence between two categorical variables — exactly what this test addresses. A significant result means the variables are associated; it does not prove the mechanism (discrimination, corruption, etc.).

Mechanics & Notation
H₀: variable A and variable B are independent
H₁: variable A and variable B are not independent (associated)
Expected count: Eᵢⱼ = (rowᵢ total × colⱼ total) / grand total
Test statistic: χ² = Σ (Oᵢⱼ - Eᵢⱼ)² / Eᵢⱼ
Degrees of freedom: df = (r-1)(c-1)  ··· r=rows, c=columns
For 2×2 table: df=1  ··· critical value at α=0.05: 3.841
Validity: all Eᵢⱼ ≥ 5
Goodness of Fit vs Independence

GoF: one variable, compare to a CLAIMED distribution. Independence: two variables, compare to EXPECTED under independence (computed from the data). GoF uses external probabilities (pᵢ given). Independence derives expected counts from the table margins — no external claim needed.

Worked Examples
Easy — Compute Expected Counts for a 2×2 Table

A hiring table: 40 minority candidates (30 hired, 10 not), 60 non-minority candidates (36 hired, 24 not). Compute expected counts under independence.

Totals: Row 1 (minority)=40, Row 2 (non-minority)=60. Col 1 (hired)=66, Col 2 (not hired)=34. Grand total=100. E₁₁ = (40×66)/100 = 26.4. E₁₂ = (40×34)/100 = 13.6. E₂₁ = (60×66)/100 = 39.6. E₂₂ = (60×34)/100 = 20.4. All ≥5 ✓. The expected counts represent what we’d see if hiring were completely independent of minority status — the same 66% hire rate for both groups.

Medium — Full 2×2 Test of Independence — Gender and Promotion

A promotion contingency table: Male promoted=45, male not promoted=55; Female promoted=25, female not promoted=75. Total n=200. Test independence at α=0.05.

Row totals: Male=100, Female=100. Col totals: Promoted=70, Not promoted=130. Grand=200.
